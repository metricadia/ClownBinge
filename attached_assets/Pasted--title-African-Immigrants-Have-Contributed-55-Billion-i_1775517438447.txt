  "body": "<p>On December 2, 2025, at the close of a two-hour White House Cabinet meeting, President Donald Trump told reporters that Somali immigrants in the United States are \"garbage\" who \"contribute nothing.\" He said it, by multiple contemporaneous news accounts, four times in seven seconds. The assembled Cabinet members applauded. That statement is now in the public record. So is everything else ClownBinge is about to show you.</p>\n\n<p>The <a href=\"https://www.americanimmigrationcouncil.org/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/2018/01/NAE_African_V6.pdf\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"Power of the Purse: How Sub-Saharan Africans Contribute to the U.S. Economy\" data-summary=\"A 2018 report published by New American Economy, now part of the American Immigration Council, analyzing U.S. Census data on Sub-Saharan African immigrants. The report documents earnings, tax contributions, educational attainment, STEM degree rates, and workforce participation. Full data drawn from American Community Survey records.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">New American Economy report on Sub-Saharan African contributions</a>, using data from the <a href=\"https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/acs\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"American Community Survey, U.S. Census Bureau\" data-summary=\"An annual nationwide survey conducted by the U.S. Census Bureau collecting detailed demographic, social, economic, and housing data on the American population. It is the primary source for immigrant population statistics and is publicly accessible at census.gov.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">U.S. Census Bureau's American Community Survey</a>, found that African immigrants earned $55.1 billion in 2015. Their households paid $10.1 billion in federal taxes and $4.7 billion in state and local taxes that year, leaving an estimated $40.3 billion in spending power circulating in the American economy. Those are not projections. They are tabulated from federal data that predates the president's December 2025 remarks by a decade.</p>\n\n<p>The educational record is equally documented. The same report found that 40 percent of African immigrants hold at least a bachelor's degree, making them 30 percent more likely than the overall U.S. population to reach that level of education. Of those with degrees, 33.4 percent majored in a <a href=\"https://nces.ed.gov/fastfacts/display.asp?id=92\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"STEM Degrees — National Center for Education Statistics\" data-summary=\"The National Center for Education Statistics, part of the U.S. Department of Education, tracks degree attainment by field across American higher education institutions. STEM fields include science, technology, engineering, and mathematics. Data is publicly accessible through NCES.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">STEM field</a>. The <a href=\"https://www.americanimmigrationcouncil.org/report/black-immigrants-2020/\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"Power of the Purse: Contributions of Black Immigrants in the United States, 2020\" data-summary=\"A 2020 report published by New American Economy analyzing U.S. Census American Community Survey data on Black immigrants from all countries. Documents income, tax contributions, spending power, healthcare employment, entrepreneurship, and educational attainment by country of origin.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">expanded 2020 Black immigrant report</a> documented that nearly 16 percent of African immigrants hold advanced degrees, a rate higher than the U.S.-born population overall. In 2018, Black immigrant households collectively earned $133.6 billion in income and paid $36 billion in taxes, leaving $97.6 billion in documented spending power.</p>\n\n<p>In Minnesota, where the president specifically directed his remarks, the University of Minnesota produced a study in 2024 estimating that Somali Minnesotans contribute approximately $1.5 billion annually to that state's economy. Economist Dr. Bruce Peter Corrie, using <a href=\"https://implan.com/\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"IMPLAN Economic Modeling System\" data-summary=\"IMPLAN is an economic impact modeling tool used by state agencies, chambers of commerce, universities, and legislators to estimate regional economic contributions. It calculates direct, indirect, and induced effects of economic activity.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">IMPLAN</a>, the same modeling tool used by state agencies and chambers of commerce, estimated that Somali Minnesotans generate over $500 million in household income annually, pay an estimated $67 million in state and local taxes each year, and contribute roughly $8 billion to the Minnesota economy in total economic impact. The $8 billion figure is larger than the entire economies of many Minnesota counties. ClownBinge makes no judgment about the president's intent. It notes that the figure was calculated and published before he spoke.</p>\n\n<p>The <a href=\"https://www.americanimmigrationcouncil.org/report/economic-impact-refugees-america/\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"Starting Anew: The Economic Impact of Refugees in America — American Immigration Council\" data-summary=\"A report by the American Immigration Council documenting the economic contributions of refugees in the United States, using U.S. Census data. Covers household income, tax payments, spending power, entrepreneurship rates, homeownership, and naturalization rates. Available at americanimmigrationcouncil.org.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">American Immigration Council's refugee economic impact report</a> documented that refugees, as a category, have an entrepreneurship rate that surpasses even other immigrants. In 2019, nearly 188,000 refugee entrepreneurs were operating in the United States. Somali workers in Minnesota are documented as comprising 11 percent of total workers in the animal food processing subsector and more than 15 percent of all workers in home health care services, a sector the same report identified as facing chronic labor shortages. The <a href=\"https://www.mnchamber.com/\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"Minnesota Chamber of Commerce\" data-summary=\"The Minnesota Chamber of Commerce is the state's largest business advocacy organization, representing businesses across Minnesota. It publishes economic data and policy analysis on the state's workforce and business environment. Data available at mnchamber.com.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Minnesota Chamber of Commerce</a> documented that nearly one-third of Minnesota's workforce is over age 55, and identified health care, transportation, retail, and manufacturing as sectors facing critical shortages, the precise sectors where Somali workers are documented as active.</p>\n\n<p>Nigerian Americans present a separate and documented case. The <a href=\"https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/acs\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"American Community Survey, U.S. Census Bureau\" data-summary=\"An annual nationwide survey conducted by the U.S. Census Bureau collecting detailed demographic, social, economic, and housing data on the American population. It is the primary source for immigrant population statistics and is publicly accessible at census.gov.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">American Community Survey</a> data and analysis from the <a href=\"https://www.migrationpolicy.org/\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"Migration Policy Institute\" data-summary=\"The Migration Policy Institute is a nonpartisan think tank in Washington, D.C., that analyzes international and U.S. immigration and refugee policies. It publishes research using U.S. Census and American Community Survey data. Reports are publicly available at migrationpolicy.org.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Migration Policy Institute</a> show that 59 percent of Nigerian immigrants aged 25 and older hold at least a bachelor's degree, nearly double the proportion for U.S.-born Americans. Among second-generation Nigerian Americans, a peer-reviewed study using the <a href=\"https://www.bls.gov/cps/\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"Current Population Survey — U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics\" data-summary=\"A monthly survey of about 60,000 households conducted jointly by the U.S. Census Bureau and the Bureau of Labor Statistics. It is the primary source for U.S. labor force statistics, including employment, earnings, and demographic data. Publicly accessible at bls.gov.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Current Population Survey</a> from 2009 to 2019 found that 73.5 percent were college graduates, compared to 32.9 percent of non-Hispanic whites over the same period. The group with the highest level of educational attainment in those years was second-generation Nigerian American women, at 71.1 percent holding bachelor's degrees or higher. A Nigerian immigrant reporting to the Nigerian-American economy in 2018 made more than $14 billion and paid more than $4 billion in taxes, according to reporting by the New American Economy.</p>\n\n<p>The documented Nigerian American contribution to American medicine is not statistical abstraction. It has a name, a case number, and a publication date. On September 28, 2002, <a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bennet_Omalu\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"Bennet Omalu — Nigerian-American Forensic Neuropathologist\" data-summary=\"Dr. Bennet Ifeakandu Omalu is a Nigerian-American physician, forensic pathologist, and neuropathologist who in 2002 discovered chronic traumatic encephalopathy (CTE) in an NFL player. He published his findings in the journal Neurosurgery in 2005. In 2016, the American Medical Association awarded him its Distinguished Service Award. He became a U.S. citizen in February 2015.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Dr. Bennet Omalu</a>, a Nigerian-born forensic neuropathologist working at the Allegheny County coroner's office in Pittsburgh, conducted an autopsy on former Pittsburgh Steelers center Mike Webster. Webster had died at 50. What Omalu found in Webster's brain, documented in a 2005 paper in the peer-reviewed journal <a href=\"https://academic.oup.com/neurosurgery\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"Neurosurgery — Journal of the Congress of Neurological Surgeons\" data-summary=\"Neurosurgery is the official journal of the Congress of Neurological Surgeons. It is a peer-reviewed scientific publication that has documented clinical and research advances in neurological surgery since 1954. The Omalu CTE paper was published in its July 2005 edition.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Neurosurgery</a>, was the first documented case of chronic traumatic encephalopathy in an NFL player. That discovery, which the NFL spent seven years attempting to discredit, ultimately forced a Congressional acknowledgment, a league-wide concussion protocol overhaul, and a class-action settlement with retired players. Omalu arrived in the United States in 1994 with a World Health Organization scholarship and $250 in his pocket. He became an American citizen in February 2015. In 2016, the <a href=\"https://www.ama-assn.org/\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"American Medical Association\" data-summary=\"The American Medical Association is the largest association of physicians and medical students in the United States, founded in 1847. Its Distinguished Service Award is the organization's highest honor, recognizing meritorious service in the science and art of medicine.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">American Medical Association</a> gave him its Distinguished Service Award, its highest honor. The doctor who did not know what American football was became the reason the American football industry now tracks brain injuries.</p>\n\n<p>The workforce numbers extend beyond Minnesota and beyond medicine. The 2020 New American Economy report documented that Black immigrants, while comprising 1.3 percent of the U.S. population, make up 3.5 percent of all healthcare workers. In 2015, almost 30 percent of African immigrants in the workforce were employed in the healthcare and services industry, compared to 13 percent of the U.S.-born working population. More than 201,000 Black immigrants owned and operated their own businesses in 2018. The <a href=\"https://www.americanimmigrationcouncil.org/report/starting-anew-the-economic-impact-of-refugees-in-america/\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"Starting Anew: The Economic Impact of Refugees in America\" data-summary=\"Report by the American Immigration Council using U.S. Census data documenting the economic contributions of refugees across income, taxes, spending power, entrepreneurship, and homeownership. Published 2022.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">American Immigration Council</a> documented that Somali refugees rebuilt neighborhoods in Fargo, North Dakota, where major employers Cardinal IG and Sanford Health stated in the report that they could not have sustained or expanded operations without the influx of refugee workers. In Buffalo, New York, the same report credits refugee resettlement with reversing decades of population decline. These are the employers' own statements, in a published federal report.</p>\n\n<p>The Minneapolis Somali business community has earned what <a href=\"https://www.mnhs.org/mnopedia\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"MNopedia — Minnesota Encyclopedia, Minnesota Historical Society\" data-summary=\"MNopedia is an online encyclopedia of Minnesota history published by the Minnesota Historical Society. It documents the history and contributions of communities across Minnesota using primary and archival sources. Accessible at mnhs.org/mnopedia.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">MNopedia</a>, the Minnesota Historical Society's encyclopedia, describes as an international reputation for successful small-scale entrepreneurship. Somali-owned trucking fleets operate across the continental United States. Somali clinicians operate medical practices in Minneapolis and St. Cloud. Somali nurses and home-health aides are documented as supporting Minnesota's aging population in a state that, by its own Chamber of Commerce data, cannot fill those roles from its native-born workforce alone. Minnesota's Teacher of the Year in 2020 was a Somali educator. Somali principals lead public schools in the Twin Cities. Somali Americans serve in city councils, the state legislature, school boards, and the United States Congress. That last point is itself documented: one Somali American represents Minneapolis in the U.S. House of Representatives, a fact the president referenced in the same Cabinet meeting where he delivered his assessment of the community's worth.</p>\n\n<p>ClownBinge has reviewed the data against the statement. The statement is: \"They contribute nothing.\" The data is: $8 billion to Minnesota's economy, $55.1 billion in documented income from African immigrants in a single year, $36 billion in taxes paid in 2018, 59 percent bachelor's degree attainment among Nigerian immigrants, the discovery of CTE, the stabilization of the Buffalo housing market, the filling of 11 percent of Minnesota's food processing workforce, and the construction of a concussion safety apparatus now used across professional and amateur sports worldwide. ClownBinge makes no judgment about the discrepancy between these two things. That is left to the record.</p>\n\n<p>The record was compiled before December 2, 2025. It will exist after it.
