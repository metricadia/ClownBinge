That statement is in the public record. It has a date. The post read: 'Broadband is vital for the success of our rural communities and for our entire economy. Great to see Alabama receive crucial funds to boost ongoing broadband efforts.' The article he linked announced that Alabama would receive $1.4 billion in broadband expansion funding. The source of that $1.4 billion was the <a href='https://broadbandusa.ntia.gov/funding-programs/bead' class='cb-factoid' data-title='BEAD Program — Broadband Equity, Access and Deployment' data-summary='The Broadband Equity, Access and Deployment Program, administered by the National Telecommunications and Information Administration, was created by the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act of 2021. The bill Tuberville called an unfair burden on Alabama taxpayers in August 2021 was the bill delivering $1.4 billion to Alabama in June 2023. ClownBinge has reviewed both posts. They are from the same senator. They describe the same funding. They reach opposite conclusions.</p><p>The self-own here is structural, but it acquired a second layer that most self-owns do not. On June 28, 2023, the official account of the President of the United States replied to Tuberville's broadband post with four words: 'See you at the groundbreaking.' President Biden did not stop there. Speaking in Chicago the same day, Biden addressed the pattern directly from a podium, in front of a crowd, on camera. 'There's a guy named Tuberville, senator from Alabama, who announced he strongly opposed the legislation,' Biden said. 'Now he's hailing its passage.' The crowd laughed. The speech is in the public record. The applause is audible on the recording. The <a href='https://www.c-span.org' class='cb-factoid' data-title='C-SPAN — Cable-Satellite Public Affairs Network' data-summary='C-SPAN is a nonprofit public service network created in 1979 by the cable television industry. This is a lesson some elected officials never learn.</p><p>Tuberville's spokesman Steven Stafford provided a statement to multiple outlets on June 28, 2023. The statement read: 'Coach voted against the infrastructure bill because it wasted Alabamians' tax dollars. It spent too much to get too little in return for Alabama. But now that it is law of the land, the people of Alabama deserve their fair share. Coach is proud to advocate for this funding to go to Alabama.' ClownBinge makes no judgment about the sincerity of this position. ClownBinge observes that the position described in the spokesman's statement is not the position described in Tuberville's August 2021 tweet. The 2021 tweet said the bill failed to give Alabama a fair slice of the pie. The 2023 spokesman statement said Alabama deserves its fair share. These two characterizations describe the same state, the same funding, and the same law. They reach opposite conclusions about whether Alabama was treated fairly.</p><p>The numbers in the record require direct attention. The White House released a fact sheet on August 10, 2021, the same day as the Senate vote, documenting what the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act would deliver specifically to Alabama. The fact sheet stated that 18 percent of Alabama households had no internet subscription and that 11.5 percent lived in areas without broadband infrastructure. It projected a minimum of $100 million in broadband funding for Alabama and stated the bill would deliver service to at least 565,000 Alabamians. That document was publicly available on the day Tuberville voted no. The final allocation Alabama received was not $100 million. It was $1.4 billion. The <a href='https://www.ntia.gov' class='cb-factoid' data-title='NTIA — National Telecommunications and Information Administration' data-summary='The National Telecommunications and Information Administration is an agency of the U.S. Department of Commerce. The distribution happened because the bill passed. The bill passed 69 to 30. Tuberville was one of the 30.</p><p>Former Republican National Committee Chairman Michael Steele, appearing as a political analyst for MSNBC, addressed Tuberville's June 2023 post directly on social media. Steele wrote: 'Umm, Coach, didn't you vote against the $1.4 billion in federal funding to expand access to the internet statewide? And now you're saying, Great to see Alabama receive crucial funds? Clearly they weren't that crucial because YOU VOTED AGAINST THEM.' That response came not from a Democrat, not from a progressive advocacy organization, and not from the White House. It came from a former chairman of the Republican National Committee. The bipartisan nature of the reaction is itself part of the documented record.</p><p>Tuberville, a former college football coach who won a national championship at Auburn University before entering the Senate in 2021, has described expanding broadband access to unserved populations in rural communities as his top priority in his role as ranking member of the <a href='https://www.agriculture.senate.gov' class='cb-factoid' data-title='Senate Agriculture Subcommittee on Rural Development and Energy' data-summary='The Senate Committee on Agriculture, Nutrition and Forestry oversees federal agricultural, food, and rural development policy. Expanding broadband access to unserved populations in rural communities is also what the Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act funded. Tuberville voted against that bill on August 10, 2021. He described it as his top priority on or around June 27, 2023. Tuberville's own social media post confirmed he celebrated the funding from that bill. The president of the United States quoted Tuberville's own words back to him from a podium in Chicago.
