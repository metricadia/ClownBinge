The record says what it says. ClownBinge has reviewed it.</p>

<p><a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Battani\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"Al-Battani — Kitab az-Zij as-Sabi (c. 900 CE)\" data-summary=\"Al-Battani's major work, the Kitab az-Zij as-Sabi (The Sabian Astronomical Tables), compiled around 900 CE, contains 57 chapters of solar, lunar, and planetary data. It was translated into Latin by Plato of Tivoli between 1116 and 1138 CE and printed in Nuremberg in 1537. Copernicus, Tycho Brahe, Kepler, Galileo, and Edmund Halley all cited al-Battani's observations directly.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Al-Battani's <em>Kitab az-Zij as-Sabi</em></a>, compiled around 900 CE at his private observatory in Raqqa, Syria, calculated the solar year at 365 days, 5 hours, 46 minutes, and 24 seconds (al-Battani, c. 900/1537). The modern accepted value differs from that figure by 2 minutes and 22 seconds, an error margin of approximately 0.00045 percent. His measurement of the Sun's eccentricity was more accurate than the values later obtained by both Copernicus and Tycho Brahe (Wikipedia contributors, 2025a). His tables were used by the German mathematician <span class=\"metricadiaid-marker\" data-metricadiaid-name=\"Christopher Clavius\" data-metricadiaid-image=\"https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/twentysix/Christoph_Clavius.jpg/440px-Christoph_Clavius.jpg\" data-metricadiaid-desc=\"Christopher Clavius (1538–1612) was a German Jesuit mathematician and astronomer who led the commission that reformed the Julian calendar, producing the Gregorian calendar adopted in 1582. His calculations relied in part on al-Battani's astronomical tables.\" data-metricadiaid-attribution=\"Wikipedia / Wikimedia Commons\" role=\"button\" tabindex=\"0\">Christopher Clavius</span> to reform the Julian calendar, producing the Gregorian calendar adopted in 1582 and still in use today (Wikipedia contributors, 2025a). The calendar by which the Western world currently marks time was corrected using the work of a Muslim scholar from 9th-century Syria.</p>

<p>The question of how Islamic scholarship reached Copernicus, Clavius, Kepler, and Galileo is not a matter of inference. It is a matter of documented translation history. In the 8th century CE, the <span class=\"metricadiaid-marker\" data-metricadiaid-name=\"Caliph Harun al-Rashid\" data-metricadiaid-image=\"https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Harun_al-Rashid_and_the_Barber.jpg/440px-Harun_al-Rashid_and_the_Barber.jpg\" data-metricadiaid-desc=\"Harun al-Rashid (763–809 CE) was the fifth Abbasid caliph, ruling from 786 to 809 CE. He founded the library that would become the Bayt al-Hikma (House of Wisdom) in Baghdad and initiated the systematic collection of manuscripts from Greek, Persian, and Sanskrit sources.\" data-metricadiaid-attribution=\"Wikipedia / Wikimedia Commons\" role=\"button\" tabindex=\"0\">Caliph Harun al-Rashid</span> established in Baghdad a royal library and academic institution that his son <span class=\"metricadiaid-marker\" data-metricadiaid-name=\"Caliph al-Mamun\" data-metricadiaid-image=\"https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Al-Mamun.jpg/440px-Al-Mamun.jpg\" data-metricadiaid-desc=\"Al-Mamun (786–833 CE) was the seventh Abbasid caliph, ruling from 813 to 833 CE. He expanded the Bayt al-Hikma into a major intellectual center and dispatched diplomatic missions to the Byzantine Empire specifically to acquire Greek scientific manuscripts for translation into Arabic.\" data-metricadiaid-attribution=\"Wikipedia / Wikimedia Commons\" role=\"button\" tabindex=\"0\">Caliph al-Mamun</span> expanded into what is now documented as the <a href=\"https://www.britannica.com/place/Bayt-al-Hikmah\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"Bayt al-Hikma (House of Wisdom) — Encyclopaedia Britannica\" data-summary=\"The Bayt al-Hikma was the royal library and intellectual center of the Abbasid caliphate in Baghdad, founded under Caliph al-Mansur (754–775 CE) and expanded under Harun al-Rashid and al-Mamun. It served as the primary institutional hub for the translation of Greek, Persian, and Sanskrit manuscripts into Arabic from the 8th through 9th centuries. The library was destroyed during the Mongol sack of Baghdad in 1258 CE.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Bayt al-Hikma</a>, or House of Wisdom, in Baghdad (Britannica, 2024). Al-Mamun dispatched diplomatic missions to the Byzantine Emperor specifically to acquire Greek manuscripts as a condition of peace treaties (Middle East Eye, 2024). More than 800 works of ancient Greek literature were procured under peace terms signed with the Byzantine Emperor Theophilus (Middle East Eye, 2024). The Abbasid caliphs preferred manuscripts as war booty over gold. The documentary record confirms this preference in multiple contemporary Arabic sources.</p>

<p>Among the scholars working at the Bayt al-Hikma was <span class=\"metricadiaid-marker\" data-metricadiaid-name=\"al-Khwarizmi\" data-metricadiaid-image=\"https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/Al-Khwarizmi_portrait.jpg/440px-Al-Khwarizmi_portrait.jpg\" data-metricadiaid-desc=\"Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi (c. 780–850 CE) was a Persian polymath and mathematician who served as astronomer and head of the library at the Bayt al-Hikma in Baghdad. His treatise Al-Jabr, written around 820 CE, is the founding document of algebra as an independent discipline, and his name is the direct etymological origin of the word algorithm.\" data-metricadiaid-attribution=\"Wikipedia / Wikimedia Commons\" role=\"button\" tabindex=\"0\">al-Khwarizmi</span>, appointed around 820 CE as astronomer and head of the library of the Bayt al-Hikma (Wikipedia contributors, 2025b). Between 813 and 833 CE, al-Khwarizmi compiled <a href=\"https://archive.org/details/aljabar_alkhwarizmi\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"Al-Jabr (The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing, c. 820 CE) — Internet Archive\" data-summary=\"Al-Khwarizmi's foundational algebraic treatise, written around 820 CE in Baghdad. A unique Arabic manuscript copy is held at the Bodleian Library, Oxford. A Latin translation by Robert of Chester (1145 CE) is held at Cambridge. The text served as the principal mathematics textbook of European universities until the 16th century. The English word algebra derives directly from al-jabr in its title.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\"><em>Al-Kitab al-Mukhtasar fi Hisab al-Jabr wal-Muqabala</em></a>, translated into English as <em>The Compendious Book on Calculation by Completion and Balancing</em> (Wikipedia contributors, 2025b). The word algebra is not a Latin word. It is not a Greek word. It is the Arabic word al-jabr, lifted directly from the title of al-Khwarizmi's manuscript, unchanged, into every European language that uses it (al-Battani, c. 900/1537; Wikipedia contributors, 2025b). The word algorithm derives from the Latinized form of al-Khwarizmi's own name, Algoritmi (Wikipedia contributors, 2025b). Every student who has ever taken an algebra class, written a line of code, or used a search engine is operating on infrastructure that carries a Muslim scholar's name in its etymology.</p>

<p>A unique Arabic manuscript of al-Khwarizmi's algebra treatise is held at the <a href=\"https://www.bodleian.ox.ac.uk\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"Bodleian Library — University of Oxford\" data-summary=\"The Bodleian Library is the main research library of the University of Oxford, established in its current form in 1602. It holds one of the world's most significant collections of manuscripts, including the unique Arabic copy of al-Khwarizmi's Al-Jabr, translated into English by F. Rosen in 1831. The library is a legal deposit library for the United Kingdom.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Bodleian Library at Oxford</a> (Wikipedia contributors, 2025b). A Latin translation is held at Cambridge. <span class=\"metricadiaid-marker\" data-metricadiaid-name=\"Robert of Chester\" data-metricadiaid-image=\"https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Liber_algebrae_et_almucabola.jpg/440px-Liber_algebrae_et_almucabola.jpg\" data-metricadiaid-desc=\"Robert of Chester (fl. 1140s CE) was an English scholar who traveled to Spain and translated al-Khwarizmi's algebra treatise from Arabic into Latin in 1145 CE at Segovia, producing the Liber algebrae et almucabala. His translation introduced systematic algebra to medieval European scholarship.\" data-metricadiaid-attribution=\"Wikipedia / Wikimedia Commons\" role=\"button\" tabindex=\"0\">Robert of Chester</span> translated al-Khwarizmi's text into Latin in 1145 CE at Segovia, under the title <em>Liber algebrae et almucabala</em> (Wikipedia contributors, 2025b). That Latin translation served as the principal mathematics textbook of European universities until the 16th century (Muslim Heritage, 2019). Not a supplementary text. Not a reference. The principal textbook. For four centuries of European mathematical education, the assigned reading was the translated work of a Muslim mathematician from 9th-century Baghdad.</p>

<p>The mechanism by which Islamic scholarship entered European universities in organized, institutional volume is documented and named. It is called the <a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toledo_School_of_Translators\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"Toledo School of Translators — 12th–13th century, Toledo, Spain\" data-summary=\"The Toledo School of Translators refers to the organized scholarly effort in Toledo, Spain during the 12th and 13th centuries to translate scientific, philosophical, and medical texts from Arabic and Hebrew into Latin. Operating under the patronage of Archbishop Raymond of Toledo (1125–1152) and later King Alfonso X of Castile, translators produced hundreds of Latin editions of Greek and Islamic works that became foundational to European university curricula. The school is considered a primary transmission mechanism for the Islamic Golden Age into European scholarship.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Toledo School of Translators</a>, operating under Archbishop Raymond of Toledo beginning in the 1120s CE (Wikipedia contributors, 2025c). Toledo had been a center of Islamic learning for centuries before its reconquest by Alfonso VI of Castile in 1085 CE. When Christian scholars arrived, they found libraries. They found libraries full of Arabic manuscripts containing the preserved and expanded works of Aristotle, Galen, Euclid, Ptolemy, and hundreds of Islamic scholars who had corrected, extended, and built upon those Greek foundations (Grokipedia, 2026). <span class=\"metricadiaid-marker\" data-metricadiaid-name=\"Gerard of Cremona\" data-metricadiaid-image=\"https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Gerard_of_Cremona.jpg/440px-Gerard_of_Cremona.jpg\" data-metricadiaid-desc=\"Gerard of Cremona (c. 1114–1187 CE) was an Italian scholar who traveled to Toledo specifically to access Arabic manuscripts. He translated 87 books from Arabic into Latin, including Ptolemy's Almagest, Aristotle's Physics and On the Heavens, Euclid's Elements of Geometry, al-Khwarizmi's algebra treatise, and Avicenna's Canon of Medicine. He is the most productive translator in the history of Western culture.\" data-metricadiaid-attribution=\"Wikipedia / Wikimedia Commons\" role=\"button\" tabindex=\"0\">Gerard of Cremona</span>, an Italian scholar who traveled to Toledo because he could not find Ptolemy's <em>Almagest</em> anywhere in Latin-speaking Europe, translated 87 books from Arabic into Latin before his death in 1187 CE (Wikipedia contributors, 2025c). Eighty-seven books. That single translator's output includes Ptolemy's <em>Almagest</em>, Aristotle's <em>Physics</em>, <em>On the Heavens</em>, <em>On Generation and Corruption</em>, and <em>Meteorology</em>, Euclid's <em>Elements of Geometry</em>, al-Khwarizmi's algebra treatise, and the foundational medical text of the entire European university system (Wikipedia contributors, 2025c).</p>

<p>That medical text is the <a href=\"https://www.loc.gov/item/2021667335/\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"Canon of Medicine (Al-Qanun fi al-Tibb) — Library of Congress\" data-summary=\"Ibn Sina's Canon of Medicine, completed in 1025 CE, is a five-volume medical encyclopedia that became the standard medical textbook at European universities for over six centuries. A manuscript copy is held in the Library of Congress World Digital Library collection. It was translated into Latin by Gerard of Cremona in the 12th century and remained in use at the University of Montpellier as late as 1650 CE.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\"><em>Al-Qanun fi al-Tibb</em></a>, or <em>Canon of Medicine</em>, completed in 1025 CE by the Persian physician and philosopher <span class=\"metricadiaid-marker\" data-metricadiaid-name=\"Ibn Sina\" data-metricadiaid-image=\"https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Ibn_Sina_-_2.jpg/440px-Ibn_Sina_-_2.jpg\" data-metricadiaid-desc=\"Ibn Sina (980–1037 CE), known in the Latin West as Avicenna, was a Persian polymath and physician who authored over 450 works across medicine, philosophy, mathematics, and astronomy. His Canon of Medicine remained the standard medical textbook at European universities for over six centuries, used at Montpellier as late as 1650 CE. Medieval Western scholars called him the Prince of Doctors.\" data-metricadiaid-attribution=\"Wikipedia / Wikimedia Commons\" role=\"button\" tabindex=\"0\">Ibn Sina</span>, known in Latin Europe as Avicenna (Library of Congress, n.d.). Ibn Sina wrote the <em>Canon</em> in five books, systematically synthesizing Greek, Roman, Persian, Chinese, and Indian medical traditions while integrating his own documented clinical observations (Wikipedia contributors, 2025d). The <em>Canon</em> was translated from Arabic into Latin by Gerard of Cremona in the 12th century and became the standard medical textbook across European universities for more than six centuries (Library of Congress, n.d.). At the <a href=\"https://www.britannica.com/topic/The-Canon-of-Medicine\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"Canon of Medicine — Encyclopaedia Britannica\" data-summary=\"The Canon of Medicine (Al-Qanun fi al-Tibb) is a five-volume medical encyclopedia completed by Ibn Sina (Avicenna) in 1025 CE. It served as the primary medical reference at European universities including Montpellier and Louvain as late as 1650 CE. Britannica documents it as one of the most influential works in the history of medicine.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">University of Montpellier</a>, one of Europe's oldest medical schools, it was still assigned reading in 1650 CE, more than six centuries after it was written (Britannica, 2024; PMC, 2013). Six centuries. That is longer than the United States has existed.</p>

<p>The self-own here is structural. The contemporary political argument that Islamic civilization is incompatible with Western intellectual values requires, as a foundational premise, that Islamic civilization made no foundational contribution to Western intellectual values. The primary source record does not support that premise. It contradicts it on every axis simultaneously. The word the West uses for its foundational mathematical discipline is Arabic. The astronomer Copernicus cited 23 times in the book that launched modern astronomy is Muslim. The medical textbook that trained European physicians for six centuries was written by a Muslim scholar in Bukhara. The translation pipeline that delivered Aristotle, Euclid, and Ptolemy back to Europe ran through Islamic Spain. These are not contextual footnotes to Western intellectual history. They are load-bearing structural facts. ClownBinge makes no judgment about the political arguments currently being made. The documents make the argument on their own.</p>

<p>The translation pipeline itself requires documentation beyond a single institution, because its scale is often underestimated. <span class=\"metricadiaid-marker\" data-metricadiaid-name=\"Hunayn ibn Ishaq\" data-metricadiaid-image=\"https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Hunain_ibn_Ishaq_al-Ibadi.jpg/440px-Hunain_ibn_Ishaq_al-Ibadi.jpg\" data-metricadiaid-desc=\"Hunayn ibn Ishaq (808–873 CE) was a Nestorian Christian Arab physician and scholar who served as chief translator under Caliph al-Mamun at the Bayt al-Hikma in Baghdad. He translated more than 100 Greek medical texts into Arabic and Syriac, including works by Galen, Hippocrates, and Dioscorides, and documented his translation methodology in a systematic bibliography.\" data-metricadiaid-attribution=\"Wikipedia / Wikimedia Commons\" role=\"button\" tabindex=\"0\">Hunayn ibn Ishaq</span>, the chief translator under Caliph al-Mamun, translated more than 100 Greek medical texts into Arabic and Syriac, including the majority of Galen's surviving corpus (Gutas, 1998). He documented his own translation methodology in a systematic bibliography, one of the earliest examples of translation theory as a formal discipline. The translation movement he led was not incidental to Islamic civilization. It was state-sponsored, institutionally organized, and conducted at a scale that had no parallel in the contemporary Latin West (Gutas, 1998). While the Latin West was restricted to the fragments of Aristotle's logical writings, Islamic scholars were working with Aristotle's complete corpus, correcting it, commenting on it, and extending it into new domains of inquiry.</p>

<p>The commentaries produced by Islamic scholars on Aristotle were not marginal annotations. They became required reading at European universities. <span class=\"metricadiaid-marker\" data-metricadiaid-name=\"Ibn Rushd\" data-metricadiaid-image=\"https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Averroes_Cordova.jpg/440px-Averroes_Cordova.jpg\" data-metricadiaid-desc=\"Ibn Rushd (1126–1198 CE), known in the Latin West as Averroes, was an Andalusian Muslim philosopher and physician who produced extensive commentaries on Aristotle's complete works. His commentaries became mandatory texts at the University of Paris and other European universities, earning him the title The Commentator in Western scholastic tradition.\" data-metricadiaid-attribution=\"Wikipedia / Wikimedia Commons\" role=\"button\" tabindex=\"0\">Ibn Rushd</span>, known in Latin Europe as Averroes, produced commentaries on Aristotle's complete works from his base in Cordoba, al-Andalus (Wikipedia contributors, 2025e). European scholastics called him simply <em>The Commentator</em>, a title reserved for one scholar across the entire Latin tradition. <span class=\"metricadiaid-marker\" data-metricadiaid-name=\"Thomas Aquinas\" data-metricadiaid-image=\"https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Saint_Thomas_Aquinas.jpg/440px-Saint_Thomas_Aquinas.jpg\" data-metricadiaid-desc=\"Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274 CE) was an Italian Dominican friar and Catholic theologian whose systematic theology, the Summa Theologica, is considered the foundational text of Catholic intellectual tradition. Aquinas built his theological framework directly on Aristotelian philosophy as transmitted through Averroes's Arabic commentaries and the Toledo translation movement.\" data-metricadiaid-attribution=\"Wikipedia / Wikimedia Commons\" role=\"button\" tabindex=\"0\">Thomas Aquinas</span>, the foundational theologian of Catholic intellectual tradition, built the Aristotelian framework of the <em>Summa Theologica</em> directly on Averroes's commentaries as transmitted through Toledo (SIL in Eurasia, n.d.). The philosophical architecture of Catholic doctrine as it exists today was constructed, in documented part, on the work of a 12th-century Muslim philosopher from Islamic Spain.</p>

<p>The astronomical record extends beyond al-Battani. <span class=\"metricadiaid-marker\" data-metricadiaid-name=\"Nasir al-Din al-Tusi\" data-metricadiaid-image=\"https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Nasir_al-Din_Tusi.jpg/440px-Nasir_al-Din_Tusi.jpg\" data-metricadiaid-desc=\"Nasir al-Din al-Tusi (1201–1274 CE) was a Persian polymath and astronomer who founded the Maragheh Observatory in northwestern Iran in 1259 CE. He saved thousands of manuscripts from the Mongol destruction of Baghdad by relocating them to Maragheh, and his Tusi couple mathematical device was later used without attribution in Copernicus's De Revolutionibus.\" data-metricadiaid-attribution=\"Wikipedia / Wikimedia Commons\" role=\"button\" tabindex=\"0\">Nasir al-Din al-Tusi</span>, anticipating the Mongol destruction of Baghdad, moved thousands of manuscripts to the <a href=\"https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maragheh_observatory\" class=\"cb-factoid\" data-title=\"Maragheh Observatory — Northwestern Iran, founded 1259 CE\" data-summary=\"The Maragheh Observatory was founded in 1259 CE in northwestern Iran by Nasir al-Din al-Tusi under the patronage of Mongol ruler Hulagu Khan. It became the largest astronomical research institution of the medieval Islamic world and housed thousands of manuscripts rescued from the Mongol sack of Baghdad. The mathematical device known as the Tusi couple, developed at Maragheh, appears in Copernicus's De Revolutionibus.\" target=\"_blank\" rel=\"noopener noreferrer\">Maragheh Observatory</a> in northwestern Iran in 1259 CE (Middle East Eye, 2024). A geometric device al-Tusi developed at Maragheh, now documented in the scholarly literature as the Tusi couple, appears in Copernicus's <em>De Revolutionibus</em> (Wikipedia contributors, 2025f). Copernicus did not cite al-Tusi for this device. Al-Battani he cited 23 times. Al-Tusi's geometric contribution he used without attribution. The De Revolutionibus Wikipedia contributors entry for the heliocentric model documents that it made use of the Urdi Lemma developed by the 13th-century Arab astronomer Mu'ayyad al-Din al-Urdi (Wikipedia contributors, 2025f). The Copernican Revolution, the foundational event of Western scientific modernity, was built on a documented stack of Islamic astronomical work.</p>

<p>The medical record is equally precise. By 1025 CE, when Ibn Sina completed the <em>Canon of Medicine</em>, the Islamic world had already produced a documented tradition of clinical medicine that European institutions would not independently replicate for centuries. <span class=\"metricadiaid-marker\" data-metricadiaid-name=\"al-Razi\" data-metricadiaid-image=\"https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Rhazes_%28al-Razi%29_-_MS_P_284.2_-_Wellcome_Images.jpg/440px-Rhazes_%28al-Razi%29_-_MS_P_284_2_-_Wellcome_Images.jpg\" data-metricadiaid-desc=\"Muhammad ibn Zakariyya al-Razi (854–925 CE), known in Latin as Rhazes, was a Persian physician who served as chief physician at the Baghdad hospital and the Ray hospital. He wrote the first documented clinical distinction between smallpox and measles, and his encyclopedic medical work Kitab al-Hawi was translated into Latin and used in European university curricula.\" data-metricadiaid-attribution=\"Wikipedia / Wikimedia Commons\" role=\"button\" tabindex=\"0\">Al-Razi</span>, known in Latin Europe as Rhazes, produced the first documented clinical distinction between smallpox and measles in the 9th century CE, a differential diagnosis European medicine would not systematically apply for centuries after his death (PMC, 2009). His encyclopedic work <em>Kitab al-Hawi</em> was translated into Latin and incorporated into European university medical curricula alongside Ibn Sina's <em>Canon</em> (PMC, 2009). The two primary textbooks of European medical education for the medieval and early modern periods were both written by Muslim physicians from Persia. This is not a marginal data point. It is the documented institutional record of European medical education.</p>

<p>ClownBinge has reviewed the full primary source stack. The Arabic unique manuscript of al-Khwarizmi's algebra treatise sits in Oxford. The Latin translation sits in Cambridge. The <em>Canon of Medicine</em> sits in the Library of Congress. <em>De Revolutionibus</em>, with its 23 citations of al-Battani, sits in the Library of Congress. The Toledo translation records, the Gerard of Cremona <em>Vita</em> and <em>Commemoratio librorum</em> compiled by his students after his death, document 87 specific titles transmitted from Arabic into Latin. The Gregorian calendar, the algebra curriculum, the astronomical tables, the medical textbooks, the Aristotelian philosophy underlying Catholic doctrine. The record is not a footnote to Western civilization. The record is a foundational layer of it.</p>
