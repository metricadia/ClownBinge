It includes internal documents, third-party research, testimony from civil rights organizations, and the prepared statements of three of the most powerful men in the history of communications technology. The documents submitted to that hearing did not arrive by accident. They arrived because Congress had already determined, based on available evidence, that the platforms those men ran had become the primary distribution infrastructure for demonstrably false information in the United States. That determination was not editorial. It was a formal finding of the legislative branch of the federal government. ClownBinge begins there, because the receipts begin there.</p><p>The scale requires precise numbers before any argument proceeds. <a href='https://www.pewresearch.org/journalism/fact-sheet/social-media-and-news-fact-sheet/' class='cb-factoid' data-title='Pew Research Center — Social Media and News Fact Sheet, 2025' data-summary='The Pew Research Center is a nonpartisan American think tank that conducts public opinion polling, demographic research, and social science research. The full fact sheet is publicly available at pewresearch.org.' target='_blank' rel='noopener noreferrer'>Pew Research Center data from August 2025</a> shows that 38 percent of U.S. adults regularly get news on Facebook, 35 percent on YouTube, 20 percent on Instagram, 20 percent on TikTok, and 12 percent on X. Among adults under 30, 43 percent now regularly get news from TikTok, up from 9 percent in 2020. That is a 378 percent increase in four years in a single demographic. The same Pew data shows that 55 percent of TikTok users overall now regularly consume news on the platform, up from 22 percent in 2020. These are not fringe behaviors. These numbers describe the primary news infrastructure for tens of millions of Americans. The infrastructure is not regulated. It is not editorially accountable. Its economics are not aligned with accuracy. Its design is not optimized for truth.</p><p>That last sentence is not an assertion. It is a description of how the platforms were built and how they have operated. The <a href='https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/tech-news/facebook-knew-radicalized-users-rcna3581' class='cb-factoid' data-title='Facebook Papers — NBC News Coverage, October 2021' data-summary='The Facebook Papers refers to a set of internal Facebook documents disclosed to the Securities and Exchange Commission and provided in redacted form to Congress by whistleblower Frances Haugen in September 2021. They showed that Facebook employees had documented the platforms role in radicalization and the spread of misinformation, and that internal warnings had been ignored. NBC News published its analysis on October 26, 2021.' target='_blank' rel='noopener noreferrer'>Facebook Papers, disclosed to the SEC and Congress by whistleblower Frances Haugen in September 2021</a>, documented that Facebook's own internal researchers had identified the platform's recommendation algorithms as pushing users into radicalization pipelines. A simulated Facebook account created by researchers in 2019 and 2020 to test the platform's recommendation behavior became, within one week, a feed described by the researcher as a barrage of extreme, conspiratorial, and graphic content. The account had expressed no extremist interest. The algorithm provided it anyway. A 2021 internal Facebook report concluded that the power of community, when based on harmful topics or ideologies, potentially poses a greater threat to our users than any single piece of content, adversarial actor, or malicious network. That sentence was written by Facebook's own employees. It was not written by ClownBinge.</p><p>Facebook's CEO testified before Congress in March 2021 that Facebook was committed to fighting misinformation and had built an industry-leading fact-checking program working with 80 independent third-party fact-checkers. The Facebook Papers, disclosed six months later, showed that internal employees had documented a consistent gap between the company's public commitments and its operational behavior. The pattern is now documented across multiple congressional proceedings, multiple whistleblower disclosures, and multiple independent research reviews. ClownBinge makes no judgment about the personal sincerity of any executive's congressional testimony. ClownBinge observes that the internal documents and the public statements describe two different companies. The internal documents were written by people with no audience to perform for. The congressional testimony was not.</p><p>YouTube's disinformation architecture operates differently but produces documented parallel outcomes. A longitudinal analysis of YouTube's promotion of conspiracy videos, submitted to the <a href='https://docs.house.gov/Committee/Calendar/ByEvent.aspx?EventID=111407' class='cb-factoid' data-title='U.S. House Hearing — Disinformation Nation, March 25, 2021' data-summary='On March 25, 2021, the U.S. House Energy and Commerce Committee convened a hearing titled Disinformation Nation: Social Medias Role in Promoting Extremism and Misinformation. Written testimony, documents submitted for the record, and responses to questions for the record are publicly available at docs.house.gov.' target='_blank' rel='noopener noreferrer'>House Energy and Commerce Committee as part of the March 2021 hearing record</a>, documented the platform's recommendation system as a systematic driver toward progressively more extreme content. YouTube is the most widely used social media platform in the United States, with <a href='https://www.pewresearch.org/internet/2024/01/31/americans-social-media-use/' class='cb-factoid' data-title='Pew Research Center — Americans Social Media Use 2025' data-summary='The Pew Research Center conducts an annual survey of U.S. adults on social media usage. The 2025 report, based on surveys conducted February through June 2025 of more than 5,000 adults, found that 84 percent of U.S. adults report ever using YouTube, making it the most widely used social media platform in the country. The full report is available at pewresearch.org.' target='_blank' rel='noopener noreferrer'>84 percent of U.S. adults reporting they use the platform</a> and 73 percent of American teenagers visiting it daily. Nine in ten American teens use YouTube. Its recommendation engine determines what those users see next after every video they watch. That engine is not curated by editors. It is optimized by an algorithm whose primary metric is watch time. Accurate content and inaccurate content are equivalent inputs. The algorithm does not distinguish between them. It rewards whatever keeps the viewer watching longest. That is not an editorial judgment. It is an engineering specification.</p><p>TikTok presents a structurally distinct problem. Its algorithm is not primarily driven by social connection or existing networks. It is a cold-start recommendation engine that serves content to users based almost entirely on behavioral signals, with no requirement for prior relationship to the content creator. A user who has expressed no political interest can be served political content within minutes. A user who has expressed no health interest can be served health misinformation within the same session. <a href='https://pirg.org/edfund/articles/misinformation-on-social-media/' class='cb-factoid' data-title='U.S. PIRG Education Fund — Misinformation on Social Media Report' data-summary='The U.S. PIRG Education Fund is a nonprofit consumer advocacy organization. The report is available at pirg.org.' target='_blank' rel='noopener noreferrer'>An investigation by The Guardian found that when TikTok users searched for top mental health videos, more than half of the results contained misinformation.</a> TikTok's U.S. user base is 63 percent teenagers. The platform reaches a population that has not yet developed the media literacy frameworks that adult users, imperfectly, possess. The misinformation does not announce itself. It arrives in the same format, with the same production value, and with the same algorithmic authority as accurate content. There is no label distinguishing the two.</p><p>X presents a documentation problem that is specific to its ownership history. Prior to October 2022, Twitter had internal trust and safety teams, content moderation policies, and a verified account system that required identity confirmation before granting the blue checkmark. After Elon Musk completed his $44 billion acquisition of the platform in October 2022, the company eliminated approximately 80 percent of its workforce, disbanded its trust and safety council, and converted the blue verification checkmark into a paid subscription product available to any account willing to pay eight dollars per month. The downstream effect of that last change is documented in peer-reviewed research. <a href='https://pirg.org/edfund/articles/misinformation-on-social-media/' class='cb-factoid' data-title='Indiana University Study — X Misinformation Concentration, 2024' data-summary='A 2024 study by Indiana University researchers analyzed the concentration of low-credibility content on X. Their misinformation carried a blue checkmark. The checkmark previously indicated identity verification. It now indicates a monthly payment. The distinction is not cosmetic. It is structural.</p><p>The business model underlying all four platforms is the same and it is documented in their own public filings. Each platform generates revenue through advertising. Advertising revenue is a function of user engagement. User engagement is maximized by content that produces strong emotional responses. Research across multiple institutions has documented that false or misleading content consistently generates stronger emotional responses and higher engagement rates than accurate content. The platforms' economic incentive, therefore, is structurally misaligned with accuracy. This is not an accusation. It is a description of how advertising-supported attention economies function. The platforms have disclosed this model in their <a href='https://www.sec.gov/cgi-bin/browse-edgar?action=getcompany&company=meta+platforms&type=10-K&dateb=&owner=include&count=10' class='cb-factoid' data-title='SEC EDGAR — Meta Platforms Annual Filings' data-summary='The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission requires public companies to disclose material risks and business operations in annual 10-K filings. They have listed misinformation as a risk factor to their own business. They have not described a structural solution because a structural solution would require modifying the engagement optimization that generates their revenue.</p><p>ClownBinge is a public accountability journalism platform. Our standard is the verifiable document. On the question of social media and disinformation, the verifiable documents are not sparse. They are abundant. They include congressional testimony, SEC disclosures, internal corporate research made public through whistleblower proceedings, peer-reviewed academic studies, and the platforms' own risk disclosures. None of those documents were produced by critics of the platforms. They were produced by the platforms themselves, by the researchers they employed, by the lawmakers who called their executives to testify, and by the scientists who studied their outputs. ClownBinge has reviewed them. The conclusion they support is not complicated. Four companies have built the largest information distribution infrastructure in human history. That infrastructure is optimized for engagement, not accuracy. The gap between those two things is where disinformation lives. The documents know this. They have known it for years.</p><p>The receipts on this one were not hard to find.
