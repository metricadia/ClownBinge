They have been reviewed by criminologists, sociologists, and public health researchers for decades. Neither dataset has ever published the claim that Black Americans are inherently more criminal than white Americans. That claim does not appear in either dataset because neither dataset supports it. The <a href='https://ucr.fbi.gov/ucr-statistics-their-proper-use' class='cb-factoid' data-title='FBI — Proper Use of UCR Statistics' data-summary='The FBI publishes official guidance on the proper use of UCR statistics. That sentence is on the FBI's website. It has been there for years. The UCR collects data on crimes reported to law enforcement. It does not collect data on crimes that were not reported. Nearly half of all serious violent crimes in the United States are never reported to police, according to the NCVS. What the UCR measures is therefore not crime. It is reported crime. A jurisdiction that deploys more police officers in a specific neighborhood will produce more arrests in that neighborhood. More arrests in that neighborhood will produce higher crime statistics for that neighborhood. The crime statistics will then be used to justify deploying more police officers to that neighborhood. The cycle is documented. The FBI's data does not control for it. Anyone who cites UCR arrest data by race without acknowledging this is not reading the document correctly. They are reading one column of a table that has many other columns.</p><p>Now to the intraracial crime finding, which is the most consistently omitted fact in American public discourse about crime and race. White Americans are most frequently victimized by white Americans. Black Americans are most frequently victimized by Black Americans. Hispanic Americans are most frequently victimized by Hispanic Americans. This is not a finding about any single racial group. It is a universal pattern documented across all groups. The reason is not complicated. Crime follows geography. Geography in the United States follows residential segregation. Residential segregation follows historical policy. People commit crimes against people who live near them. People who live near them are statistically likely to share their race. The intraracial crime pattern is not a pathology of any single community. It is the documented mathematical outcome of how American residential patterns were structured over more than a century of policy.</p><p>The FBI's own homicide data documents that approximately 80 to 81 percent of white homicide victims were killed by white offenders in recent years.
