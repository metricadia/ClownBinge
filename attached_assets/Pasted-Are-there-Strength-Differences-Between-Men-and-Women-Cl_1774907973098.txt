The question has obvious political charge. That charge has, in practice, made honest answers harder to find. People wanting to establish hierarchy cite the data selectively. People wanting to dispute hierarchy also cite the data selectively. The primary sources support neither agenda. They support a more complicated picture.

The short version: men and women differ in measurable ways across several strength domains. In others, the differences are smaller than widely assumed. In some domains, women hold the clear advantage. Understanding which is which requires reading the actual research.

Muscular Strength: Upper Body, Lower Body, and What the Numbers Mean
The clearest and largest gap in the literature involves upper body muscular strength. Miller et al. (1993) analyzed muscle fiber composition and maximal force production in men and women matched for physical activity. Men produced significantly greater absolute upper body force, with women approximately 40 to 60 percent weaker depending on the specific task and muscle group tested.

Lower body strength showed a smaller gap. The same analysis found women at approximately 25 to 30 percent below men in lower limb force production (Miller et al., 1993). When researchers controlled for lean body mass, the gap narrowed substantially. It did not disappear, but the scale of difference changed. The architecture of muscle fiber does not differ meaningfully between sexes. The difference is in how much of it you have and where it is distributed.

Lauber et al. (2014) confirmed the pattern using electromyography and force-plate measurement across a range of lower-limb tasks. Absolute differences are real. Relative differences, expressed per kilogram of lean mass or per unit of muscle cross-section, are considerably smaller. Both measurements are valid. They answer different questions.

Grip Strength: The Biomarker That Cuts Both Ways
Bohannon (2019) conducted a large normative analysis of grip strength across the lifespan in men and women. Men averaged approximately 45 kilograms of grip force. Women averaged approximately 25 kilograms. That is a significant gap by any standard measurement.

Grip strength matters beyond physical contests. It is one of the most consistent predictors of all-cause mortality, cardiovascular disease risk, and cognitive decline in aging populations (Bohannon, 2019). The muscle system functions as an organ system, and its output is a metabolic health signal. Men's higher grip strength in midlife does not translate into longer lives. It translates into a number on a dynamometer. The longevity data points in the opposite direction entirely, as discussed below.

Endurance: Where the Physical Gap Closes
Aerobic capacity, measured as VO2 max, is higher in men on average. Expressed in absolute liters of oxygen per minute, men outperform women substantially. When adjusted for lean body mass, the gap shrinks. When adjusted for hemoglobin mass, which drives oxygen transport, it shrinks further (Tarnopolsky, 2008).

At the metabolic level, Tarnopolsky (2008) demonstrated that women oxidize fat at a higher rate during submaximal endurance exercise. Men rely more heavily on carbohydrate stores at equivalent intensities. At ultramarathon distances, where glycogen depletion becomes a primary limiting factor, this metabolic difference is functionally significant. Women's relative performance improves as distances extend beyond the marathon.

The performance gap in running events narrows progressively from sprint to ultramarathon distances. At sprint distances, the gap is largest. Beyond 100 miles, multiple analyses have found it approaches a few percentage points. The metabolic machinery operates differently at extended distances. That difference is not in one direction only.

Immune System Strength: A Consistent Female Advantage
Klein and Flanagan (2016) published a comprehensive review in Nature Reviews Immunology documenting sex-based differences in immune function. Women consistently mount stronger innate and adaptive immune responses. Antibody titers following vaccination are higher in women across multiple vaccine types. Immune cells proliferate more aggressively in response to pathogens. The evidence across this domain is consistent and large in effect size.

The tradeoff is equally well-documented. Approximately 80 percent of autoimmune disease cases occur in women (Ngo et al., 2014). Conditions including lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, multiple sclerosis, and Hashimoto's thyroiditis affect women at rates two to nine times higher than men, depending on the condition. The immune system that fights infection more aggressively also misfires more often. Strength and stability are not the same attribute.

Gubbels Bupp et al. (2018) connected this pattern to sex hormone signaling. Estrogen upregulates immune gene expression, including genes implicated in autoimmune pathology. This is not a design flaw in any meaningful sense. It is a set of biological tradeoffs with consequences operating in both directions simultaneously.

Longevity: The Largest Strength Gap Nobody Discusses
Globally, women outlive men by approximately five to seven years. This pattern holds across virtually every country and every era of recorded demographic data (Austad & Fischer, 2016). It is one of the most consistent findings in human biology and one of the least discussed in popular conversations about sex differences.

Austad and Fischer (2016) reviewed the mechanistic evidence. Proposed explanations include mitochondrial inheritance patterns, the cardioprotective effects of estrogen prior to menopause, lower baseline levels of oxidative stress in females, and behavioral differences in risk-taking and healthcare utilization. No single mechanism accounts for the full gap. The finding itself is not contested by anyone working in the field.

Men die at higher rates from cardiovascular disease, cancer, accidents, suicide, and infectious disease at virtually every age. The lifespan gap is not a statistical artifact of one cause of death. It is a broad and durable biological signal that cuts across every major category of mortality.

Pain: Threshold Versus Tolerance Versus Context
Fillingim et al. (2009) conducted a systematic review of experimental and clinical pain research. In laboratory settings, women report lower pain thresholds and higher pain ratings for equivalent stimuli across multiple modalities including heat, pressure, cold, and electrical stimulation. This finding is frequently cited to conclude that women have lower pain tolerance overall.

The clinical picture is more complicated. Fillingim et al. (2009) documented that women also report higher rates of chronic pain conditions, which may reflect genuine physiological differences in pain processing, differences in reporting norms, or both. Pain threshold in a controlled laboratory experiment and functional pain tolerance under real-world demands are not the same variable. The researchers explicitly cautioned against conflating them.

The broader biological context matters here. Childbirth, for the entirety of human history prior to anesthesia, required sustained pain endurance at intensities no controlled experiment has measured or replicated in a laboratory setting. What threshold tests capture is one narrow and highly specific slice of the pain system.

Cognition: The Similarities Hypothesis Dominates
Hyde (2005) published the gender similarities hypothesis after conducting a meta-analysis covering 46 prior meta-analyses on cognitive, social, and behavioral variables. The conclusion was unambiguous: men and women are more similar than different on most measured psychological attributes. Effect sizes for most cognitive differences are small, and within-group variation dwarfs between-group differences across every domain examined.

Specific domains show consistent, if modest, directional differences. Voyer et al. (1995) conducted a meta-analysis of spatial ability research and found that men outperform women on mental rotation tasks with a medium effect size. This is one of the more replicable cognitive differences in the published literature.

Women show advantages in verbal memory, processing speed on certain tasks, and fine motor precision (Weiss et al., 2003). Linn and Petersen (1985) documented female advantages in spatial visualization tasks involving objects embedded in complex visual contexts, which cuts against the simple narrative that men hold uniform visuospatial superiority. The domain matters. Spatial ability is not one thing.

Effect sizes in cognitive sex difference research are almost universally small. The practical overlap between male and female score distributions on any cognitive measure is large. Knowing a person's sex tells you almost nothing predictive about their cognitive performance on any individual task, because the distributions sit mostly on top of each other.

The Variance Pattern
One finding that receives less popular attention than it deserves involves variance rather than means. Across multiple domains, male score distributions show greater spread. More men appear at the extreme high and extreme low ends of tested trait distributions, while women's scores cluster more tightly around the mean (Hyde, 2005).

This pattern, sometimes called the greater male variability hypothesis, does not establish superiority. It means that the tails of distributions differ in composition. This has potential implications for rates of extreme performance and extreme pathology, but it says nothing about typical capability for the vast majority of individuals in the middle of any distribution. The averages and the tails are separate questions.

What the Research Actually Supports
The evidence establishes genuine differences in specific domains. Men hold a clear advantage in absolute upper body strength. Women hold clear advantages in immune response robustness and longevity. Endurance differences narrow with distance and metabolic context. Cognitive differences exist in isolated domains and are consistently small in effect size. Pain research requires careful attention to what specific measures are capturing.

What the data does not support is any global ranking of one sex as stronger. The question has no coherent answer unless you specify stronger at what. A mean difference in grip strength and a five-to-seven-year lifespan gap point in different directions. Both are real. Both are in the data. Researchers who have spent careers on this literature have largely converged on that conclusion.

The primary sources are accessible and specific. The picture they produce resists the simplicity that most popular discussions demand of it.
