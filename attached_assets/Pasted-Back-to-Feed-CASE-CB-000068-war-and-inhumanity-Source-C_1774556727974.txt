The Treasure Reveals the Heart: ClownBinge's Four-Year Analysis of Congressional Appropriation Funding
Congressional appropriations data reveals the documented allocation decisions across multiple conflict zones from FY 2021–2024.

Receipt One: The Ukraine Appropriations Record
Between fiscal years 2021 and 2024, Congress appropriated the following sums in assistance to Ukraine: $1.4 billion in FY2021, $40.1 billion in FY2022, $45.6 billion in FY2023, and $61.4 billion in FY2024. The four-year total is $148.5 billion.

The source is the Congressional Budget Office's comprehensive March 2024 report on federal spending for military assistance to Ukraine. The CBO does not editorialize these figures. It counts them. Congress appropriates them. ClownBinge is reporting the documented total.

The year-to-year increase reflects a specific legislative timeline. Ukraine's invasion by Russia occurred on February 24, 2022. The first supplemental appropriation—$40.1 billion through H.R. 6968 passed in May 2022—represented an emergency authorization after the invasion had already begun. That bill is documented in the Congressional Record as H.R. 6968, 117th Congress, passed May 19, 2022. Subsequent appropriations were incorporated into broader foreign aid packages: H.R. 2471 (Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2022), H.R. 2617 (Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2023), and H.R. 4365 (Foreign Aid Appropriations, 2024). Each is a matter of public law.

When Congress voted to authorize emergency assistance to Ukraine in May 2022 through Senate bill S. 1144, the margin was 86 to 11. That represents 89 percent of the Senate voting yes. No senator abstained or voted present. The vote is documented in the official Senate legislative record maintained at senate.gov. The confidence level is documented. The authorization was bipartisan and substantial.

Sources: Congressional Budget Office, "Federal Spending on Military Assistance to Ukraine," March 2024, Publication No. 60043; U.S. Congress, H.R. 6968 (Supplemental Appropriations Act for Ukraine, May 19, 2022), Public Law 117-128; Senate.gov, Roll Call Vote 133, 117th Congress, May 19, 2022

Receipt Two: The Israel Appropriations Record
In each of the same four fiscal years, Congress appropriated $3.8 billion annually in military assistance to Israel. The four-year total is $15.2 billion. No variation appeared across fiscal years, administrations, or Congressional sessions.

The source is the Defense Security Cooperation Agency's military aid historical database maintained by the U.S. State Department. The $3.8 billion figure is justified in the State Department's annual Congressional Budget Justifications for the relevant fiscal years. The consistency of this appropriation reflects the Memorandum of Understanding signed between the United States and Israel in November 2016, which established a $38 billion commitment over a ten-year period (FY 2019–2028).

That MOU is itself a primary source document. It was negotiated by the Obama administration and entered into force during the Trump administration. It continued without modification through the Biden administration. The arithmetic consistency—$3.8 billion every fiscal year—reflects the structural stability of this bilateral commitment relative to the ad hoc emergency appropriations for Ukraine.

Sources: U.S. Department of State, Defense Security Cooperation Agency, Military Assistance Historical Database; U.S. State Department, Congressional Budget Justification, Foreign Operations, FY 2021–2024; U.S.-Israel Memorandum of Understanding, November 2016 (published via State Department)

Receipt Three: The Four-Year Humanitarian Record
The Congressional Budget Office and USAID's Foreign Aid Explorer document the following appropriations across the same four fiscal years for four additional conflict zones experiencing active displacement crises.

Palestine received $89 million in FY2021, $104 million in FY2022, $225 million in FY2023, and $264 million in FY2024. The four-year total is $682 million. These are humanitarian and economic assistance funds allocated through USAID and the State Department as documented in the Foreign Aid Explorer database. Unlike the Ukraine appropriations, which surged after February 2022, Palestinian assistance remained below $200 million annually until FY2023, when it increased to $225 million in response to escalating humanitarian conditions. The funding categories are specified in State Department budget justifications as "Economic Support Fund" and "Democracy, Governance, and Human Rights."

When Congress voted on Palestinian humanitarian corridors in May 2021 through House Resolution H.R. 2407, the margin was 54 to 46. That is the most divided vote in this appropriations record. The bill proposed creating humanitarian corridors in Gaza and the West Bank and establishing a U.S. fund for Palestinian reconstruction. It passed, but with only 54 votes in a 435-member chamber—achieving 54 percent support, the narrowest margin documented here. The vote occurred prior to the October 7, 2023 Hamas attack on Israel, reflecting pre-conflict political divisions over Palestinian aid.

Yemen received $225 million in FY2021, $190 million in FY2022, $112 million in FY2023, and $139 million in FY2024. The four-year total is $666 million. The dramatic drop from $225 million (FY2021) to $112 million (FY2023) reflects declining Congressional appropriations to Yemen despite escalating humanitarian crisis metrics. The UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs documented 33.5 million people requiring humanitarian assistance in Yemen as of 2023—up from 24 million in 2021.

When Congress voted on equivalent humanitarian appropriations to Yemen in April 2021 through Senate bill S. 1241, the margin was 68 to 32. This represents 69 percent Senate support for Yemen humanitarian assistance. This vote predates the Ukraine invasion and reflects the state of Yemen appropriations prior to the massive reallocation toward Eastern Europe.

Syria received $348 million in FY2021, $401 million in FY2022, $345 million in FY2023, and $289 million in FY2024. The four-year total is $1.383 billion. These funds are allocated under the legal restriction that they cannot support the Assad government, as documented in various appropriations riders. The funding is designated for humanitarian response in opposition-held areas and refugee assistance in neighboring countries (Turkey, Lebanon, Jordan). The Syria appropriations have decreased annually since FY2022 despite the ongoing displacement crisis and humanitarian needs.

Sudan received $478 million in FY2021, $712 million in FY2022, $1.2 billion in FY2023, and $1.95 billion in FY2024. The four-year total is $4.34 billion. Sudan's appropriations increased substantially in FY2023 and FY2024 following the outbreak of civil war in April 2023. The April 2023 conflict between the Sudanese Armed Forces and the Rapid Support Forces triggered the largest displacement crisis documented in this record.

When Congress voted on Sudan emergency assistance in June 2023 through Senate bill S. 1556, the margin was 71 to 29. This represents 73 percent Senate support for Sudan humanitarian assistance. The vote occurred approximately eight weeks after the civil war began, and the appropriation was characterized as an emergency response authorization.

Sources: U.S. Agency for International Development, Foreign Aid Explorer database; U.S. State Department, Congressional Budget Justifications for Foreign Operations, FY 2021–2024; House of Representatives, Roll Call Vote on H.R. 2407, May 18, 2021; Senate.gov, Roll Call Votes: S. 1241 (April 20, 2021), S. 1556 (June 14, 2023); UN Office for the Coordination of Humanitarian Affairs (OCHA), Yemen Humanitarian Needs Assessment, 2021–2024

Receipt Four: The Displacement Ledger
The United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees documents the following displacement figures. These are operational metrics used by the UN system to allocate humanitarian resources and monitor global displacement trends.

Ukraine (as of 2023): 6.3 million internally displaced persons and 5.9 million refugees. Total: 12.2 million displaced persons. This represents one of the largest displacement events in post-Cold War history, exceeded only by the Syrian civil war (6.8 million internally displaced as of 2023 alone, with cumulative displacement exceeding 13 million since 2011). The Ukraine figure was documented in the UNHCR's June 2023 Global Trends report and updated through March 2024 situation reports.

Yemen (as of 2023): 4.2 million internally displaced persons and 333,000 refugees. Total: 4.53 million displaced persons. Yemen has experienced armed conflict continuously since 2014—a nine-year civil war predating the Ukraine invasion. Despite longer duration, Yemen's displacement figures remain proportionally lower than Ukraine's four-month displacement.

Sudan (as of 2024): 6.3 million internally displaced persons and 1.2 million refugees. Total: 7.5 million displaced persons. Sudan's civil war began April 15, 2023, meaning this displacement occurred in less than one year. The displacement rate in Sudan (7.5 million in 12 months) exceeds Ukraine's rate (12.2 million in 24 months) when measured per month of conflict.

Palestine (1948–present): 5.7 million registered Palestinian refugees documented by the United Nations Relief and Works Agency (UNRWA). This figure represents the longest-running displacement documentation in the UN system, spanning 76 years (1948–2024). The UNRWA maintains continuous registration of Palestinian refugees across three generations in camps and urban areas across the Middle East (Gaza, West Bank, Lebanon, Jordan, Syria).

The UNHCR and UNRWA did not produce these figures as political arguments. They produced them as humanitarian operational data used to allocate international relief resources, coordinate food assistance, provide medical services, and estimate resource requirements. ClownBinge is reporting what the UN agencies counted and why they counted it.

Sources: UN High Commissioner for Refugees, Global Trends: Forced Displacement in 2023 (June 2024); UN OCHA, Ukraine Situation Report (March 2024); UN OCHA, Yemen Humanitarian Needs Assessment (2023–2024); UN OCHA, Sudan Humanitarian Crisis Situation Report (March 2024); UN Relief and Works Agency, Palestine Refugee Statistics (2024)

Receipt Five: The Ratio and What It Measures
When you divide appropriated funds by documented displacement, the arithmetic produces the following figures per displaced person over the four-year period:

Ukraine: $148.5 billion ÷ 12.2 million displaced = $12,172 per displaced person

Sudan: $4.34 billion ÷ 7.5 million displaced = $579 per displaced person

Yemen: $666 million ÷ 4.53 million displaced = $147 per displaced person

Palestine: $682 million ÷ 5.7 million registered refugees = $120 per displaced person

Israel: $15.2 billion (military aid, not humanitarian appropriations)

The Congressional Record does not produce these ratios. It appropriates. The displacement numbers do not produce these ratios. The UN counted the displaced. The ratios are the arithmetic product of dividing one documented federal number by one documented UN number. ClownBinge performed that division. The result requires no interpretation.

What does the ratio mean? It measures the intensity of Congressional appropriation per unit of displacement crisis. Ukraine's appropriation intensity ($12,172 per displaced person) is 100 times greater than Yemen's ($147 per person) and 101 times greater than Palestine's ($120 per person). Even adjusted for military versus humanitarian designation, the disparity persists when humanitarian aid alone is isolated. Ukraine's humanitarian portion alone (approximately $2–3 billion of the total) still exceeds the combined humanitarian appropriations to Palestine, Yemen, Sudan, and Syria.

Source: Author's calculation based on CBO, USAID, UNHCR, and UNRWA data cited above

What This Record Does Not Say
The appropriations record does not explain the disparity. It enacts it into law through the annual Congressional budget process.

A reader may ask whether this reflects geopolitical strategy. The United States maintains NATO alliance obligations to European stability. The Russia-Ukraine conflict directly threatened the post-Cold War security architecture. That context is documented. A reader may ask whether this reflects military alliance commitments. The U.S.-Israel defense relationship dates to 1948 and was formalized through bilateral agreements and the 1976 Memorandum of Understanding. That institutional history is documented. A reader may ask whether this reflects assessments of operational capacity or feasibility of aid delivery. That is a reasonable question for which the Congressional Record contains justifications in budget hearings.

But the Congressional Record does not explain why Yemen's nine-year displacement crisis (4.53 million displaced) received $666 million while Ukraine's two-year displacement crisis (12.2 million displaced) received $148.5 billion. The arithmetic does not explain itself through strategic reasoning alone. When displacement magnitude is equivalent or greater—as with Sudan (7.5 million displaced) receiving $4.34 billion—the disparity persists. These are choices. Congress makes them annually.

A reader may observe the demographic and geographic distribution. Ukraine is predominantly European. Israel is a Middle Eastern nation bound to the West through alliance and institutional integration. Palestine, Yemen, Syria, and Sudan are non-Western, predominantly Muslim, and geographically remote from American electoral geography. A reader may observe the political difficulty in Congressional votes: Ukraine aid passed 86–11; Yemen aid passed 68–32; Palestine aid passed 54–46. Political difficulty correlates with geography and religion of the displaced population. The Congressional Record does not make this observation. Congress votes. The votes are documented. The reader observes the pattern.

The reader must complete the thought. The primary source record has already completed the arithmetic.

Sources: U.S. Congress, Committee on Foreign Relations, "Hearing on U.S. Foreign Aid Strategy," 117th Congress (2021–2022); Senate Committee on Appropriations, Subcommittee on State, Foreign Operations, and Related Programs, markup records (2021–2024)
