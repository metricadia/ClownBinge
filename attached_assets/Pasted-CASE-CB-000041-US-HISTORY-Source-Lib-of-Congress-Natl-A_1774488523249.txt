The United States Was Built by Indigenous Nations, Enslaved Africans, and Immigrants. The Primary Source Record Confirms Each.
A primary source accounting of what immigrants, enslaved Africans, Indigenous First Nations, and the native peoples of the Southwest built, cultivated, and contributed to America. The record is long because their contributions are long. It is not a matter of debate.

The United States has, at intervals, been seized by a political conviction that immigrants and outsiders represent a threat to the American way of life. The documentation tells a different story. Not a story of sentiment. Not a story of aspiration. A story of verified, primary-source-confirmed fact about who built this country, whose labor made its wealth possible, whose knowledge fed its people, and whose land it was before any of the current debates about belonging began.

This report is that record. It is long because the record is long. It is not complete, because no single document could be. What it is, is accurate. Every claim in this article is traceable to primary documentation. ClownBinge encourages readers to check every source listed at the bottom of this page.

The First Nations: Who Was Here Before the Border
Hundreds of Indigenous nations lived here before the United States existed, each with its own government, economy, language, trade system, and body of scientific knowledge. That predates the charters, the arrivals, the flag plantings. The United States did not emerge on empty land. The land had been farmed, mapped, and governed for thousands of years before that happened.

They built farms. Corn, beans, squash, potatoes, tomatoes. Hundreds of other crops, too. It fed more than colonial America. It fed the world. European colonists took maize from the Americas and moved it into Africa, Asia, and Europe through trade routes. It became a staple crop. South American growers cultivated potatoes in the Andes. They did this for centuries. It ended famine cycles across Europe. Cacao. Vanilla. Avocado. Chili peppers. Tobacco. Rubber. The crops feeding most of the planet were domesticated by Indigenous peoples in the Americas. The Smithsonian National Museum of the American Indian catalogued it. So did the USDA Agricultural Research Service.

The Iroquois Confederacy had representative governance. Historians and legal scholars have traced its influence on the framers. The documentation exists. The formal name is Haudenosaunee. The Great Law of Peace joined six nations into the Confederacy. It spelled out representation rules, consensus requirements, executive limits, and member nation rights. Franklin cites the Iroquois model in his 1751 Observations Concerning the Increase of Mankind. In 1988, Congress passed Resolution 331. The record states the Iroquois Confederacy influenced the Constitution. The bill is still posted on congress.gov.

The highway system follows trail networks built by Indigenous nations. The colonists did not build it. The Eastern Seaboard route that became U.S. Route 1, the Appalachian trade routes, the paths that became western corridors: same pattern. They were inherited. The country kept the names: Mississippi, Massachusetts, Connecticut, Illinois, Iowa, Kansas, Michigan, Minnesota, Missouri, Nebraska, Ohio, Tennessee, Wisconsin. Hundreds of American cities, rivers, mountains, and lakes still carry their Indigenous names. The people now framed in immigration debates as latecomers named most of the geography of the United States.

Modern medicine borrowed its pharmacological foundation from Indigenous peoples of the Americas. That is the documented record. Willow bark. That's where aspirin comes from. They used it for pain and swelling. The record goes back centuries. European medicine isolated the salicylate chemistry. The result became the most widely used drug in human history. The first drug to work against malaria came from the cinchona tree. That was quinine. Modern antimalarials descend from it. Andean Quechua used it for fever. The World Health Organization puts it at 25 percent. One in four pharmaceutical drugs now in clinical use came from plant compounds that Indigenous medical traditions identified first. The National Institutes of Health National Library of Medicine holds it. All of it.

The Labor That Built the Economy: African Enslaved People
The early American economy ran on slave labor. The record is unambiguous: millions of enslaved African men, women, and children built it under force of law. They received no wages. They held no legal standing. They died without recognition of their labor. The wealth generated by that labor did not disappear when slavery ended. Banks ran on it. Insurance companies did too. Cotton mills. Railroads. Global trade.

Enslaved people built the White House and the Capitol. This is not contested. The White House Historical Association put it in print. The Architect of the Capitol, too. So did the National Archives. The ledgers show payments. They were made. The workers received nothing. The money went to the white men who owned them. Enslaved labor built both buildings. They had no legal right to be anywhere their owners did not place them.

The antebellum South grew cotton that powered early U.S. commerce and fed the mills of European industrialization. By 1860, the United States grew two-thirds of the world's cotton. The cotton ended up in Massachusetts textile mills and in England. It generated foreign exchange that financed American infrastructure and development. The 1860 census valued enslaved people higher than every railroad and factory in the country added together. They were not paid for the labor, the knowledge, the agricultural skill. Or the lives. The country was built on it regardless. The Federal Writers Project Slave Narratives sit in the Library of Congress. 2,300 people on the record. They remain.

The record documents individual Black Americans, enslaved and free, who built American science, culture, architecture, and industry. The catalog is vast. Rillieux invented the multiple-effect evaporator in the 1840s. He was a free Black engineer from New Orleans. It changed sugar refining worldwide. It still runs the evaporation lines in sugar refineries, soap plants, gelatin factories, and condensed milk operations across six continents. Morgan was Black, and he invented the three-position traffic signal and the gas mask. Woods held over 60 patents. Many covered railroad electrical systems, foundational work in the field. Matzeliger was born in Dutch Suriname. He immigrated to Massachusetts and invented the lasting machine. It mechanized shoe production. Working-class Americans could suddenly afford shoes. The file sits at the Smithsonian National Museum of African American History and Culture. The U.S. Patent and Trademark Office has them on file.

American music is African music that moved here. Blues, jazz, gospel, rock and roll, hip-hop, country music: they all come from musical traditions enslaved Africans brought here and Black Americans built under generations of legal exclusion. The Smithsonian holds extensive primary documentation of this cultural record.

People Here Before the Line: Native Mexicans and the Indigenous Southwest
The irony is in the file. The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo was signed on February 2, 1848. It is still in the National Archives. Before that treaty, California, Arizona, New Mexico, Nevada, Utah, and Colorado were Mexico. Wyoming too, in part. Spain held it as colonial territory before Mexico existed. Spain showed up where the Navajo, Hopi, Pueblo, Zuni, Apache, and Comanche already lived. The same pattern shows up in forty-three other countries. They had lived there for centuries. Cultivated it. Governed it.

Mexico ceded 525,000 square miles. Sale price: $15 million. The census counted between 80,000 and 100,000 Mexican citizens in the territory. The U.S. offered them citizenship. The Indigenous peoples already living there got nothing. The line moved on the map. They stopped being people and became a management problem. Some families counted as undocumented border crossers are Indigenous people whose land the border was drawn through. Their great-grandparents were alive when the surveyors drew that line.

Acequia irrigation systems come from Indigenous water management, then Spanish and Mexican practice over centuries in the Southwest. They still irrigate farms here. The USDA Natural Resources Conservation Service put the number at 1,000 active acequia systems in New Mexico. They filed that number. Not an estimate. Some of these gravity-fed community water channels have run for more than 400 years. The engineering and agricultural tradition behind them developed entirely outside the scope of American immigration. They were here before there was a United States. They continue to feed American families today. The farmers who built and maintained them are frequently described in contemporary policy as a burden on the American agricultural system they invented.

Adobe construction, pueblo building layouts, the urban design of Santa Fe and Taos and Tucson: Indigenous and Mexican communities built those systems before the cities were American. The United States acquired the territory with its cities already operating, farms already producing, land-use laws already on the books, and trade routes already in motion. These were not American innovations. They were adopted inheritances from the peoples who were already there.

How the U.S. Pays for Things

He held the position first: Secretary of the Treasury. He designed the Bank of the United States, established the national credit system, wrote 51 of The Federalist Papers, and built the economic framework that every administration since has either used or tried to dismantle. He set the terms for debate on federal spending, the debt, and the government's role in economic institutions. Those debates still frame U.S. economic policy. The ten-dollar bill. That's where he is. He came from a Caribbean island. Most Americans could not locate it on a map if asked.

The Infrastructure of American Industry

By 1900, Carnegie Steel was producing more steel than all of Great Britain. It was the largest steel operation on earth. That steel went into American bridges, skyscrapers, railroads, and warships. He sold the company. Most of the money he gave away. The record counts 2,509 public libraries across the United States, Canada, and the English-speaking world. They still stand. Free public access to education and information, funded entirely by an immigrant from Scotland who arrived with nothing.

Tesla arrived in New York in 1884 from what is now Croatia with four cents, a letter of introduction, and engineering concepts that would reshape the physical infrastructure of the world. His alternating current system beat Thomas Edison's direct current design. It became the standard for electrical transmission. It still is. Every American home, hospital, factory, and data center draws power through a distribution system that descends directly from Tesla's designs. He died in a New York hotel room in 1943. The patent count was over 300. The bank records did not match his version. His birthplace: the Austro-Hungarian Empire. Serbian heritage. USPTO confirmed it. In his case, he was right to come.

The birth record says Bavaria. He arrived in 1847. Levi Strauss filed the patent with Jacob Davis, a Latvian tailor, in 1873. They became the most recognized symbol of American casual culture worldwide. Two immigrants invented it, the garment that now reads as American. Patent 139,121. The record is still public at the USPTO. 1873, according to the record. The documentation is not difficult to find.

Science, Medicine, and the Nuclear Age
Einstein left Germany in 1933. The Nazis had taken power. The U.S. granted asylum. He was not a citizen. He ended up at the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton. He died there. In 1939, he signed a letter to Franklin D. Roosevelt. The letter warned that Nazi Germany was working on an atomic bomb. It told the United States to build a nuclear program. The letter is still in the Roosevelt Library. It led to the Manhattan Project. The atomic age, the cold war deterrence strategy, and the trajectory of American military and scientific power for the second half of the twentieth century were shaped by this.d, in meaningful part, by a refugee physicist who had been stripped of his citizenship by the country he fled. He was an immigrant. The National Archives has the correspondence.

Kariko came to the United States in 1985. She brought $900 sewn into a stuffed animal. Hungary had a cash export limit for emigrants. Her research was used to build the COVID-19 mRNA vaccine. She spent decades at the University of Pennsylvania conducting mRNA research. It was repeatedly defunded. She was demoted. Most of the scientific establishment considered it unpromising. She and immunologist Drew Weissman developed the mRNA modification work. Pfizer-BioNTech and Moderna used it as the basis for their vaccines. The shots went into hundreds of millions of American arms. Public health agencies say the acute phase ended. The 2023 Nobel in Physiology or Medicine went to her. She immigrated. She had $900 when she arrived. The Nobel Prize Committee's citation is public record.

The American Economy, By the Numbers
The National Foundation for American Policy ran the company registration records and biographical data. Immigrants or their children founded about 45 percent of Fortune 500 companies as of 2022. They are not charities. These are the biggest U.S. corporations by revenue. They employ more workers than anyone else, pay more in taxes, and produce more economic activity. Google. Yahoo. eBay. Instagram. Zoom. WhatsApp. Tesla. Immigrants or their children founded most of the platforms, products, and services Americans use every day. The U.S. Patent and Trademark Office has them on file. The IRS holds the tax records. The SEC holds the filings. The NFAP study, using all of this data, is publicly available. The record is not ambiguous, and it has not been ambiguous for a long time.

The Physical Construction of the United States
The transcontinental railroad, completed on May 10, 1869, at Promontory Summit, Utah, was built by two workforces moving toward each other across the continent, and the Central Pacific Railroad's workforce, moving eastward from Sacramento, was composed of roughly 80 to 90 percent Chinese immigrants. They blasted through the Sierra Nevada mountains, managed nitroglycerin in conditions that killed workers regularly, and completed construction through one of the most brutal mountain passages in North America. The Union Pacific workforce, moving westward from Omaha, was composed primarily of Irish immigrants and Civil War veterans. Together they drove the golden spike that united the coasts, made national commerce possible, and compressed a weeks-long overland journey into days. The railroad transformed the American economy. The majority of the people who built it were not American-born. The Stanford University Chinese Railroad Workers in North America Project has assembled primary documentation of the Chinese workers' contributions, because for most of the century following the railroad's completion, that history was not widely taught.

The Statue of Liberty, the structure the United States has placed in New York Harbor as its physical declaration of values to the world, was designed by French sculptor Frederic Auguste Bartholdi, with its iron skeleton engineered by Gustave Eiffel. Bartholdi spent nearly two decades on the project, fundraising on both sides of the Atlantic, supervising construction in France, and crossing the Atlantic himself to select the site on Bedloe's Island in New York Harbor. Eiffel, who had not yet built the tower that bears his name, designed the internal iron armature that allows the copper exterior to flex with temperature changes without cracking. The statue has stood since 1886 as the most recognized symbol of American identity and American welcome. It was designed and built by French nationals, commissioned as a gift from France, and dedicated with a poem by Emma Lazarus, the daughter of Sephardic Jewish immigrants. The most American landmark in existence was made by people who were not American.

The question of whether immigrants, Indigenous peoples, enslaved Africans, and native populations of acquired territories contributed to the American way of life is not a philosophical question open to debate. It is a historical question with a documented answer. The answer is yes, extensively, across every sector of American life, over every period of American history, including periods when the law actively prevented those contributors from receiving credit, compensation, or citizenship.
