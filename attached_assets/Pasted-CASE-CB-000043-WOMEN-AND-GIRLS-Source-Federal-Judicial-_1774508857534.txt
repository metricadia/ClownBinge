In 1988, a senior at Miami Palmetto Senior High School in Florida wrote in her yearbook that she intended to go into law and eventually have a judicial appointment. Her name was Ketanji Brown Jackson. The Federal Judicial Center confirms she became one. The record between that yearbook entry and her seat on the Supreme Court of the United States is thirty-four years of documented public record. ClownBinge has reviewed it.

The documented career of Ketanji Brown Jackson begins at Harvard University, where she enrolled despite her guidance counselor's advice to set her sights lower. She graduated magna cum laude in 1992 with a Bachelor of Arts in Government. She entered Harvard Law School, served as supervising editor of the Harvard Law Review, and graduated cum laude in 1996. She then clerked for three federal judges across three levels of the federal judiciary: the U.S. District Court for the District of Massachusetts, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the First Circuit, and the Supreme Court of the United States, where she clerked for Associate Justice Stephen G. Breyer from 1999 to 2000. After her clerkships, she worked in private practice, served as assistant special counsel at the U.S. Sentencing Commission, served as an assistant federal public defender in the District of Columbia, and returned to the Sentencing Commission as vice chair and commissioner. This is the documented record before her first federal judicial appointment.

In September 2012, President Barack Obama nominated Jackson to the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia. At her confirmation hearing that December, Republican U.S. Representative Paul Ryan introduced her and stated for the record that his praise for her intellect, character, and integrity was unequivocal. The Senate Judiciary Committee reported her nomination by voice vote. She was confirmed by the full Senate by voice vote in March 2013. She served on the district court for eight years, issuing more than 500 written opinions. In 2021, President Joe Biden nominated her to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit. The American Bar Association rated her well qualified for that appointment. She was confirmed to the D.C. Circuit with bipartisan support. On February 25, 2022, President Biden nominated her to the Supreme Court of the United States.

Before her Supreme Court confirmation hearings began in March 2022, the ABA's Standing Committee on the Federal Judiciary completed its evaluation. The committee interviewed more than 2,000 members of the legal community and conducted confidential interviews with approximately 250 attorneys and judges with direct knowledge of Jackson's work. Its rating was unanimous. Well Qualified. That is the ABA's highest possible rating. The committee's evaluation covers three criteria only: integrity, professional competence, and judicial temperament. It does not evaluate philosophy, political affiliation, or ideology. The ABA has been evaluating Supreme Court nominees for more than sixty years. Its letter to the Senate Judiciary Committee is public record. Among the findings documented in testimony before the committee: no judge, defense counsel, or prosecutor interviewed expressed any concern about her sentencing record. One high-ranking attorney in the U.S. Attorney's office, when asked about allegations she was soft on crime, responded on the record: I vehemently disagree.

The confirmation hearings for Ketanji Brown Jackson before the Senate Judiciary Committee were held March 21 through 24, 2022, and are documented in Senate Hearing 117-422, published by the U.S. Government Publishing Office. The hearing ran across four days. Jackson answered questions for more than twenty hours. Several Republican senators focused their questioning not on her judicial philosophy as documented in more than 500 written opinions, but on a characterization of her sentencing record that the ABA's independent investigation of 250 legal professionals had already documented as contradicted by the record. Senator Josh Hawley of Missouri alleged she had been lenient in sentencing defendants convicted of child pornography offenses. Jackson's response, documented in the hearing transcript, directed the committee to the factors Congress requires judges to consider, including probation office recommendations. Data subsequently reviewed by journalists and legal analysts confirmed her sentences were consistent with federal judges nationally during the same period, including judges in Missouri.

Senator Ted Cruz of Texas, who attended Harvard Law School in the same years as Jackson, used his questioning time to display children's books and allege that a private school on whose board Jackson served promoted critical race theory. Jackson stated for the record that the academic framework, used primarily in law schools, does not come up in her work as a judge and has no bearing on her judicial record. She noted that the board of trustees does not control curricula. Cruz's Harvard Law School credential and Jackson's Harvard Law School credential are the same credential from the same institution in the same years. The hearing transcript documents both the questions and the answers. C-SPAN recorded all four days in full. The archive is public.

Senator Marsha Blackburn of Tennessee asked Jackson during the hearing to provide a definition of the word woman. Jackson responded that she could not, noting she is not a biologist. Senator Blackburn's question and Justice Jackson's answer are in the hearing transcript. They are also in the C-SPAN archive. Senator Blackburn sits on the Senate Commerce Committee and the Senate Armed Services Committee. The question she chose to ask the most credentialed female Supreme Court nominee in American history, in the two hundred and thirty-two years during which no Black woman had ever sat on that court, is in the public record. ClownBinge makes no judgment about the intent of the question. The transcript records what was asked.

The self-own here is structural. It is built into the sequence of documented events. A nominee with a magna cum laude undergraduate degree, a cum laude law degree, Harvard Law Review editorial experience, clerkships at all three levels of the federal judiciary including the Supreme Court, eight years on the federal district court, service on the U.S. Sentencing Commission, experience as a federal public defender, one year on the D.C. Circuit, and a unanimous Well Qualified rating from the American Bar Association sat before a committee and was asked to define the word woman. The document containing that exchange is Senate Hearing 117-422. It is available to any American with an internet connection.

On April 7, 2022, the Senate confirmed Ketanji Brown Jackson to the Supreme Court of the United States by a vote of 53 to 47. Three Republican senators voted in favor. She was sworn in on June 30, 2022, replacing Justice Stephen Breyer, for whom she had clerked twenty-two years earlier. She became the 116th Justice of the Supreme Court and the first Black woman in the court's two hundred and thirty-three year history to hold that seat. The Federal Judicial Center updated her record accordingly. Her judicial philosophy, her opinions, and her votes on the court are matters of ongoing public record and legitimate subjects of legal and political debate. Her credentials are not. They are in the archive.

In 1988, she wrote in her yearbook that she wanted to be a judge. She was eighteen years old. Her guidance counselor had told her to aim lower. The Federal Judicial Center does not record the guidance counselor's name. It records hers.
