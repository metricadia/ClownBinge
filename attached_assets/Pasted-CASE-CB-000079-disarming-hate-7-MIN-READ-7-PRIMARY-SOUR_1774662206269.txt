What Part One Found
The first investigation compared credentials. Department of Energy: a Nobel Prize in Physics and an MIT theoretical physicist on one side; an animal science graduate who forgot the agency existed on the other. Department of Education: a Harvard sociologist and a triple-degree holder from Harvard, Yale, and Columbia on one side; a B.A. in Business Administration who had never attended public school on the other. The full record is in Part One. The pattern across every department is the same. The administration that called DEI a threat to merit produced the demonstrably less credentialed appointees. The argument that this was about qualifications does not survive the comparison.

What Part One did not name is the system the replacement appointments represent. This article does.

WDEI: A Definition
WDEI stands for White Discrimination, Exclusion, and Ignorance. It is not an epithet. It is a description of three documented institutional practices that, taken together, constitute a functioning preferential system for white Americans that has operated continuously in American public life, that has never been targeted by an executive order, and that the anti-DEI movement has shown no interest in dismantling.

Each component has a scholarly and evidentiary record.

W: The White Affirmative Action Infrastructure
The GI Bill of 1944 is the largest affirmative action program in American history. Ira Katznelson, in the most comprehensive scholarly account of the period, documented that Black veterans were systematically excluded from its housing loans, university access, and small business provisions through a combination of segregated institutions and racially discriminatory administration. The result was a federal transfer of wealth, education, and homeownership to white families specifically. Not through private bias alone. Through program design.

Legacy admissions at elite universities are a direct heir to this system. Arcidiacono, Kinsler, and Ransom, using Harvard's own litigation-disclosed admissions data, documented that legacy status provided an admissions preference equivalent to a 45-point increase in the probability of admission, independently of academic credentials. Legacy applicants were overwhelmingly white. The executive orders that ended race-conscious admissions programs at federal contractors did not mention legacy preferences. A system that explicitly transfers advantage across generations by bloodline, disproportionately to one racial group, was preserved in its entirety. That is the W.

D: Documented Discrimination
The discrimination component of WDEI is the most extensively audited phenomenon in labor economics. Bertrand and Mullainathan submitted 5,000 identical resumes to 1,300 employers, varying only the racial connotation of the applicant's name. Resumes with white-sounding names received 50 percent more callbacks than identical resumes with Black-sounding names. Identical qualifications. The only variable was the name. The researchers found that a higher-quality resume improved outcomes more for white applicants than for Black applicants, meaning discrimination also reduced the return on merit itself.

The effect persists when applicants know about it and attempt to correct for it. Kang and colleagues, publishing in Administrative Science Quarterly, found that Black applicants who removed all racial signals from their resumes received 25.5 percent callback rates, compared to 10 percent for unaltered resumes. Identical qualifications, again. Companies with explicit diversity commitments showed no significant difference in their callback rates. The discrimination preceded DEI. It survived it. It is documented to persist now. That is the D.

E: The Architecture of Exclusion
Exclusion requires no active hostility. It requires only that the existing system continue operating. Cheryl Harris, in what became one of the most cited articles in American legal scholarship, published in the Harvard Law Review, argued that whiteness in the United States has functioned historically as a form of property, conferring enforceable rights of access and expectation. The legal refusal to account for racial identity, she documented, does not produce neutrality. It preserves whatever racial advantage had already been accumulated and treats it as the neutral baseline.

The exclusion visible in Part One's appointment data is a contemporary expression of this. The departments whose leadership shifted from career-domain experts to political and personal loyalists did not produce demographically random results. The pattern is consistent with what Harris described: a system that is not explicitly organized by race nonetheless produces racially consistent outcomes when it operates from a baseline that was itself racially constructed. That is the E.

I: The Ignorance That Preserves the System
Ignorance, in WDEI, refers to a specific and documented phenomenon: the systematic underestimation by white Americans of the racial disparities that WDEI produces and maintains. Kraus and colleagues, publishing in Perspectives on Psychological Science, found that white Americans systematically underestimated the Black-white wealth gap, believing it to be roughly half its documented size. The misperception was not random. It was directional and consistent: the problem always appeared smaller than it was. The researchers found that this systematic underestimation made it easier to oppose corrective policies because the condition being corrected had been rendered, perceptually, as nearly nonexistent.

Eduardo Bonilla-Silva has documented the ideological infrastructure that converts this ignorance into policy. Colorblind racism, as he characterizes it, does not require racial hostility. It requires only a vocabulary of neutrality: merit, individual responsibility, equal opportunity, colorblindness itself. Each principle, applied to a structurally unequal baseline, reproduces the inequality it claims not to see. The executive order that abolished DEI while leaving legacy admissions intact is a nearly textbook application of what Bonilla-Silva describes: abstract liberalism invoked to oppose the correction while preserving the condition. That is the I.

The Rhetorical Question, Answered
The question in the title of this article is rhetorical. The record answers it. If you preserve legacy admissions, you are running a preferential system for the children of people who attended institutions that were, within living memory, explicitly racially segregated. If you preserve the geographic wealth patterns created by racially explicit federal mortgage discrimination, you are the beneficiary of a government DEI program that operated from 1934 to 1968 with legal authority and federal funding. If you oppose the correction of a documented 50 percent hiring callback gap while leaving unchanged the institutional structures that produce it, you have not ended DEI. You have selected which DEI to protect.

WDEI is not an accusation that any individual is conscious of this, or intends it, or would endorse it if it were named plainly. Kraus's research suggests most would not recognize the magnitude of the disparity they are defending. Bonilla-Silva's framework suggests the ideological vocabulary of merit and colorblindness makes the system self-concealing. Harris's legal analysis suggests the baseline itself has been constructed so that whiteness reads as neutral and any correction reads as preference.

The neologism is offered not as an epithet but as a mirror. If the programs you eliminate are the ones that correct for a racial hierarchy, and the programs you preserve are the ones that perpetuate it, then merit is not the principle at work. A name for the actual principle is useful. This one fits the documented record.

Resumes with white-sounding names received 50 percent more callbacks than identical resumes with Black-sounding names. The gap was consistent across industries, occupation, and employer size. Kraus et al. (2019): The Misperception of Racial Economic Inequality, Perspectives on Psychological Science

Michael Kraus and colleagues, publishing in Perspectives on Psychological Science, documented systematic misperceptions among white Americans about the magnitude of racial economic inequality. The misperception was not random: it was directional, consistently underestimating racial inequality. The researchers found that the misperception was greater among white Americans with higher social class identification. The paper argues that these systematic misperceptions make it easier to oppose corrective policies because the problem being corrected appears smaller or nonexistent from within the misperception.
