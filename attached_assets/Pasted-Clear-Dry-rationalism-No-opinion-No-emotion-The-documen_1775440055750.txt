Philo of Alexandria Wrote About the Logos Before the Gospel of John Existed. His Definition Does Not Include a Person.
The Primary Source Record of What the Word Actually Meant to a Contemporary of Jesus

In the first century of the Common Era, a Jewish philosopher named Philo was writing in Alexandria, Egypt. He was writing in Greek. He was writing about a concept he called the Logos. He was approximately the same age as Jesus of Nazareth. The two men were contemporaries in the same historical moment, one in Egypt, one in Judea, separated by the Mediterranean coast and the width of the Sinai peninsula.
Philo wrote extensively. His works survive. They constitute the most substantial body of first-century Hellenistic Jewish philosophical writing in existence. In those works, the Logos appears dozens of times. What it means in those texts is documented, translated, and available to any researcher with access to a university library or an internet connection.
What it does not mean, in any of those texts, is a person.
I. What Philo Wrote
Philo of Alexandria, born approximately 20 BCE and active through approximately 50 CE, was the most significant Jewish philosopher of the Hellenistic world (Hillar, 2023). He wrote in Greek, read the Hebrew scriptures primarily through the Septuagint, and produced a synthesis of Platonic philosophy and Jewish monotheism that would prove foundational to subsequent Western theological development. His corpus includes On the Creation of the World, Allegorical Interpretation, On the Confusion of Tongues, On the Migration of Abraham, and dozens of additional treatises.
In those works, Philo describes the Logos as the intermediary power through which the transcendent God relates to creation. The Logos is, in his formulation, God‚Äôs first-born, the pattern after which the world was made, the rational ordering principle that stands between the absolute transcendence of God and the material world (Hillar, 2023; Todorovska, 2015). He calls it, in various passages, the image of God, the shadow of God, the instrument of creation, the high priest of the cosmos, and the divine reason.
What he does not call it is a human being. What he does not call it is incarnate flesh. What he does not call it is any specific person living in Judea during the same decades he was writing.
The Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy documents Philo‚Äôs Logos as follows: the direct agent of creation is not God himself, but the Logos (Amir et al., 2018). The Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy confirms that Philo‚Äôs Logos is a mediating divine principle, the divine mind acting through its powers, and explicitly not identified with any historical person (Hillar, 2023).
Williamson, cited in peer-reviewed scholarship on Philo‚Äôs texts, summarizes the Logos as follows: though the Logos is neither God nor a god, it is the primary, secondary layer of the effulgence or emanation of the divine light, the Thought of God, and therefore appropriately called either the second God or allowed the title theos (Todorovska, 2015, p. 47). The distinction matters. In Philo‚Äôs Greek, ho theos, meaning God with the definite article, designates the transcendent supreme being. Theos without the article designates a being with divine quality but subordinate to and distinct from the supreme God. Philo is precise about this distinction throughout his corpus.
II. The Word Before It Was Translated as Word
The Greek term logos carries a documented range of meanings in classical and Hellenistic usage. The Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy documents these meanings as including: word, speech, statement, discourse, refutation, ratio, account, explanation, and reason (Hillar, 2023). In philosophical usage, logos designated the rational principle underlying the cosmos. Heraclitus used it in the sixth century BCE to describe the rational structure of the universe. The Stoics developed it as the animating rational principle of all existence. Philo inherited both traditions and synthesized them with the Hebrew concept of God‚Äôs creative speech as documented in the opening of Genesis.
The word logos does not mean Word in the sense that the English translation implies. The English word Word refers to a unit of language, a spoken or written expression. The Greek logos, in its philosophical usage, designates reason itself, the rational principle of cosmic order, the active intelligence through which the divine relates to the material. These are not the same concept. They share a surface connection through the idea of speech and expression but diverge entirely at the philosophical level.
The translation of logos as Word in the Gospel of John entered the English language through the Latin Vulgate translation of Jerome, completed in the late fourth century CE (Hendricks, 2014). Jerome translated logos as verbum, the Latin word for word. The King James translators in 1611 rendered verbum as Word. That translation has governed English-language reading of John 1:1 ever since.
Jerome was writing after the Council of Nicaea. His translation choices reflect the theological categories that council had established.
III. The Document That Changed the Definition
The First Council of Nicaea convened in 325 CE in the city of Nicaea in what is now Turkey. It was called by the Roman Emperor Constantine to resolve a theological dispute that had fractured the Christian churches of Alexandria (Britannica, 2024). The dispute concerned the nature of Jesus and his relationship to God the Father. Arius, a clergyman of Alexandria, had argued that Jesus was a created being, subordinate to and distinct from God. His opponents argued that Jesus was coequal and coeternal with God.
The council produced the Nicene Creed, documented in its proceedings and preserved through Eusebius of Caesarea‚Äôs Ecclesiastical History and subsequent conciliar records (New Advent, 2024). The creed declares Jesus Christ to be the only-begotten of the Father, of the substance of the Father, God of God, Light of Light, very God of very God, begotten not made, of one substance with the Father.
The Greek term the council used to express this relationship was homoousios, meaning of one substance. Britannica documents that this term was non-scriptural, meaning it does not appear in the New Testament (Britannica, 2024). It is a Greek philosophical term inserted into a theological creed by a council convened by a Roman emperor to settle an institutional dispute three hundred years after the events the creed describes.
The St. Andrews Encyclopaedia of Theology documents that the Council of Nicaea represented a critical watershed between the local and often diverse theologies of one God as Trinity in the second and third century Christian communities and the universal credal statements of the ancient imperial church (St. Andrews, 2024). Before Nicaea, the documentary record shows diverse and sometimes contradictory understandings of the relationship between God and Jesus circulating among Christian communities. After Nicaea, one formulation was declared orthodox and others were declared heresy.
Arius was exiled by Constantine for refusing to sign the creed.
IV. Philo and John: The Documentary Record
The relationship between Philo‚Äôs Logos and the Logos of the Gospel of John‚Äôs prologue is a documented subject of scholarly inquiry. The Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy notes that Philo may have influenced the authors of the Gospel of John, citing C. H. Dodd‚Äôs scholarship on the subject (Hillar, 2023). The peer-reviewed journal Antiquitas Viva documents that the distinction between ho theos and theos without the article in John 1:1 precisely mirrors the distinction Philo drew between the supreme God and the Logos as second God (Todorovska, 2015).
In John 1:1 as preserved in the Greek New Testament, the text reads: en arche en ho logos, kai ho logos en pros ton theon, kai theos en ho logos. The first two uses of theos carry the definite article. The third does not. This grammatical distinction, present in the original Greek and invisible in the English translation, replicates the precise distinction Philo drew between the supreme transcendent God and the Logos as a subordinate divine principle (Todorovska, 2015).
Philo‚Äôs Logos is not a person. It is not incarnate. It is a mediating divine principle through which the transcendent God acts in creation. That is what the word meant to a Hellenistic Jewish philosopher writing in the same generation as Jesus. That is what the documentary record of his surviving texts shows.
V. The Translation History
What a first-century Hellenistic reader in Alexandria would have understood by the word logos when reading the opening of the Gospel of John is documented in Philo‚Äôs corpus. What a twenty-first century English reader understands by the word Word when reading the King James or New Revised Standard Version translation of the same verse is documented in the translation history of the Vulgate and its English descendants.
Those are two different things. The gap between them has a documented history. It includes Nicaea in 325, Constantinople in 381, Chalcedon in 451, Jerome‚Äôs Vulgate in the late fourth century, and the King James translators in 1611.
Philo died approximately 50 CE. He never read the Gospel of John. The Gospel of John was written approximately 90 to 100 CE, four decades after Philo‚Äôs death. Philo did not know what the word logos would come to mean in subsequent centuries of theological development. He knew what it meant in the first century of the Common Era, in Alexandria, in the philosophical tradition he inhabited.
His texts say what they say. The council proceedings say what they say. The translation history says what it says.
The documents are in the record.
